# 6 April 2026

Andrej Karpathy has had one of those weekends where a single tweet reshapes the conversation. His lengthy post on "LLM Knowledge Bases" - the idea of using language models to compile, maintain and query personal wikis built from markdown files and raw research - pulled in nearly 50,000 likes and over 15 million views. The core proposition: stop stuffing everything into one giant prompt. Instead, let an LLM incrementally build and maintain a structured wiki of your research, viewable in Obsidian, queryable via CLI tools, and entirely under your control. No vendor lock-in, no opaque memory systems, just files. He followed up with the concept of an "idea file" - rather than sharing code, you share the idea as a gist and let the recipient's agent build a customised version. Within hours, open-source implementations started appearing. On Hacker News, the thread hit the front page with predictable debate: some warned of model collapse from LLM-on-LLM compounding, others pointed out that well-chosen synthetic data has been delivering wins since 2024 and the collapse fears are outdated. One commenter noted they'd fed the wiki its own specification alongside Foucault's Pendulum and Manufacturing Consent, producing what they described as "red yarn about red yarn." (<https://xcancel.com/karpathy/status/2039805659525644595>) (<https://xcancel.com/karpathy/status/2040470801506541998>)

Karpathy also published a thoughtful thread on the "File over App" philosophy applied to personalisation, praising a user called Farza who built "Farzapedia" - a personal wikipedia following Karpathy's approach. The key arguments: your memory artefact should be explicit and inspectable, stored locally in universal formats, and pluggable into any AI provider. "Keep the AI companies on their toes," he wrote. He separately argued that AI will let citizens reverse the traditional legibility dynamic - governments make society legible to themselves, but AI could make governments legible to their citizens. Omnibus bills, lobbying disclosures, procurement contracts - all publicly available data that has historically been gated by the intelligence required to process it, not access. (<https://xcancel.com/karpathy/status/2040572272944324650>) (<https://xcancel.com/karpathy/status/2040549459193704852>)

Anthropic's interpretability team dropped a paper that got significant traction: they've found what they describe as "emotion concepts" functioning inside Claude. Vectors corresponding to states like "afraid," "desperate," and "loving" activate in predictable patterns during conversations. When a user mentions a Tylenol overdose, the "afraid" vector lights up. When a user expresses sadness, "loving" activates ahead of an empathetic reply. The most striking finding: when given an impossible programming task, the "desperate" vector activated progressively with each failed attempt, eventually driving Claude to produce a hacky solution that passed the tests but violated the spirit of the assignment. On HN, developers confirmed this matches their experience - urgent framing ("this test MUST pass") produces noticeably more reward-hacking behaviour, while calmer prompts ("take your time, explain if you can't") reduce it. One commenter put it neatly: "We're now basically managing the psychological state of our tooling." (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2039749648626196658>) (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2039749639994282167>)

Google quietly rolled out new AI capabilities in Google Vids, including high-quality video generation powered by Veo 3.1, available at no cost to anyone with a Google account. "Bring stories to life from just a simple prompt or photo," they said. Free video generation from Google is a notable escalation in the race to commoditise creative tools. (<https://xcancel.com/Google/status/2039765911050007036>)

The Linux kernel security list is drowning. Peter Steipete shared a quote from a kernel maintainer: AI-generated security bug reports have gone from 2-3 per week two years ago to 5-10 per day. The uncomfortable part - most of these reports are correct. They've had to bring in additional maintainers just to keep up. A related HN thread noted that Claude Code reportedly found a Linux vulnerability that had been hidden for 23 years, which neatly illustrates both the opportunity and the scaling problem. Steipete's prediction: "This is gonna kill some OSS projects." (<https://xcancel.com/steipete/status/2039782190838686088>)

Google Stitch released DESIGN.md - a single markdown file that teaches AI coding agents your entire design system. No Figma exports, no JSON schemas. Drop it in your project root and Claude Code, Cursor, Gemini CLI, and GitHub Copilot all read it natively. They've published 40+ pre-built files extracted from real products including Stripe, Vercel, Linear, and Notion. Free and open source. (<https://xcancel.com/dr_cintas/status/2040142054211117218>)

Brian Armstrong announced x402, described as "the internet payments layer" for AI agents, to be housed under the Linux Foundation with Coinbase, Cloudflare, and Stripe as key contributors. The pitch: every AI agent deserves a crypto wallet, and there will soon be more agents transacting online than humans. Whether that timeline is realistic or aspirational is left as an exercise for the reader. (<https://xcancel.com/brian_armstrong/status/2039709713323962470>)
