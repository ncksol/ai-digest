# 15 April 2026

OpenAI launched GPT-5.4-Cyber yesterday - a variant of its flagship model fine-tuned specifically for defensive cybersecurity work - and the timing could not be more pointed. As covered extensively since 8 April, Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview has been finding zero-day vulnerabilities in every major operating system and browser, prompting the formation of Project Glasswing. OpenAI's response, trailed in Friday's digest as an upcoming cybersecurity product, is now real: a model that lowers refusal boundaries for legitimate security research and adds binary reverse engineering capabilities, enabling security professionals to decompile and analyse executables directly. Reuters, WIRED, Bloomberg, Axios, CNET, 9to5Mac, and XDA all ran the story. The model is not available to the public. Access is restricted to vetted cybersecurity researchers through OpenAI's expanded "Trusted Access for Cyber" programme, which the company says it plans to scale to thousands of defenders. WIRED's framing captured the competitive dynamic: "In the Wake of Anthropic's Mythos, OpenAI Has a New Cybersecurity Model - and Strategy." As Gizmodo put it last week, OpenAI essentially raised its hand and said "we also have one of those." The difference is that Mythos found the vulnerabilities autonomously; GPT-5.4-Cyber is positioned as a tool to help humans find and patch them. Whether that distinction holds as capability improves is the question nobody wants to answer yet.

The rumoured April date for Spud - OpenAI's next-generation model, tracked in this digest since 9 April - came and went yesterday without a launch. The 14 April prediction, based on Polymarket odds and model retirement patterns, did not materialise. FindSkill.ai is now pointing at today, 15 April, as the next window, though Greg Brockman's public comments - calling Spud "two years of research" and "not an incremental improvement" - remain the only official signal. No blog post, no model card, no API announcement. Financial markets remain fixated; a FinancialContent analysis described it as "anticipation not seen since the original launch of ChatGPT." The post-training safety evaluation window from the 24 March pretraining completion would suggest late April to early May. Watch this space, but do not hold your breath for today.

Anthropic, meanwhile, has quietly restructured its entire enterprise pricing model, and the implications are significant. The Information broke the story: enterprise customers are now billed per-token for Claude, Claude Code, and Cowork usage on top of a $20 per user monthly base fee, replacing the previous flat subscription of up to $200 per user per month. Heavy users - the kind running Claude as an "unlimited AI employee," as @birdabo put it - are expected to see bills double or triple. The timing is instructive. As covered in Saturday's digest, Anthropic had already been tightening the screws on individual subscribers, with the cache TTL reduction and subscription restructuring eroding developer goodwill. This enterprise shift extends that logic: Anthropic is moving from growth-at-all-costs pricing to margins-matter pricing, driven by what The Information describes as a compute crunch. The leaked Dresser memo from OpenAI, covered on Monday, accused Anthropic of "grossing up" its run rate; Anthropic switching to usage-based billing - which is inherently more transparent - may be a partial answer to that charge. For enterprises that had budgeted on flat fees, though, the surprise is unwelcome.

The Altman security situation has escalated further. Following the Molotov cocktail attack and the Sunday shooting covered in Monday's digest, the FBI raided the Texas home of suspect Daniel Moreno-Gama on Monday. The 20-year-old from Spring, Texas, has now been charged with two counts of attempted murder (targeting both Altman and a security guard), attempted arson, and possession of a destructive device. Federal prosecutors separately charged him with attempted property destruction and possession of an unregistered firearm. The most alarming new detail: the DOJ revealed that investigators found a document on Moreno-Gama titled "Your Last Warning," which advocated for the killing of AI company CEOs and investors and listed names and addresses of multiple targets. Fortune ran it under the headline: "Sam Altman's attacker had a kill list of AI executives. Experts warn this is just the beginning." His attorney says he was experiencing an "acute mental health crisis." He appeared in San Francisco court yesterday and is being held without bail, with a return date of 5 May. The sequence - firebombing, shooting, kill list, FBI raid - marks something genuinely new in the history of anti-technology violence. On Hacker News, the thread drew comparisons to the Gilded Age, with one commenter noting: "We went through a cycle like this once before in U.S. history, and the amount of violence it took to correct the overreach of organised money was not zero."

Google DeepMind hired a philosopher. Not as a consultant or an advisory board member, but as a full-time employee with the actual job title "Philosopher." Henry Shevlin, currently Associate Director at the Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence at Cambridge, announced he will join DeepMind in May to work on machine consciousness, human-AI relationships, and AGI readiness. The hire follows Anthropic's earlier appointment of Amanda Askell as its AI philosopher, and it comes a week after this digest covered Anthropic consulting Catholic and Protestant theologians about Claude's "moral and spiritual development." The pattern is now unmistakable: the labs building the most capable AI systems are reaching outside computer science for help thinking about what they are building. NDTV, India Today, Mint, and Medium all ran the story. As Hassabis himself has said, the world needs great philosophers - invoking Kant, Wittgenstein, and Aristotle - to navigate what comes next. Whether a single philosopher on a payroll at a company racing to build AGI represents the right scale of intervention is, well, itself a philosophical question.

Hassabis was also in the news for a less elevated exchange. Steve Yegge, the former Google senior director of engineering and author of "Vibe Coding," published a viral post claiming that Google's internal AI adoption rate is comparable to John Deere's - the tractor company. Yegge cited a friend who is a 20-year Google technical director, alleging a "20/20/60" split: 20% deep AI users, 20% who refuse to use it, and 60% stuck on basic tools. He claimed Claude Code is banned internally because it is a competitor, and that Gemini has "never been good enough to make people actually use it." Hassabis responded personally and bluntly: "Maybe tell your buddy to do some actual work and to stop spreading absolute nonsense. This post is completely false and just pure clickbait." Other Google leaders piled on with longer defences. Yegge did not back down. VentureBeat covered the exchange in detail. The underlying tension is real regardless of who is right: if Google's own engineers are not enthusiastically using Google's own AI tools, that is a problem no amount of executive denial can fix.
