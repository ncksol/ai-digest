# 18 April 2026

Anthropic launched Claude Design yesterday, and Figma's stock immediately cratered.

Claude Design, powered by Claude Opus 4.7, lets users create prototypes, presentations, slides, and one-pagers through conversation. Describe what you want, get back a complete website, landing page, or pitch deck. No design tools required. (<https://xcancel.com/claudeai/status/2045156267690213649>) The product launched with a Canva integration baked in - generate ideas in Claude, edit in Canva, no friction - making it simultaneously a competitor to Figma and a distribution play through Canva's massive user base. (<https://xcancel.com/canva/status/2045163029138948493>) Ryan Mather, who works on Anthropic's verticals team serving seven different internal products, posted tips for getting the best results, noting that "an hour of setup and refinement" on your design system is worth it. His thread pulled 6,100 likes and 838,000 views. (<https://xcancel.com/Flomerboy/status/2045162321589252458>)

Figma's reaction was immediate and brutal. The stock fell over 4% within hours of the announcement, wiping roughly $700 million in market cap. @cryptopunk7213's thread (4,200 likes, 857,000 views) connected the dots: Anthropic had recently left Figma's board, and now "they built a product that not only replaces them, it's just better." (<https://xcancel.com/cryptopunk7213/status/2045162618336477427>) @brewmarkets confirmed the tumble was directly tied to the launch. (<https://xcancel.com/brewmarkets/status/2045175784554283228>) Trung Phan captured the mood with characteristic precision: "unfortunately, Claude Design is about to lead to a flood of meditation apps in the App Store." (<https://xcancel.com/TrungTPhan/status/2045189936341766302>) On Hacker News, the Claude Design thread hit 990 points and 649 comments - a rare level of engagement - with discussion centring on whether this represents genuine disruption of design tooling or another AI demo that impresses on first contact and disappoints on the tenth. Figma is already down 50% this year; Claude Design is the latest and most direct threat yet.

The launch also served as the public debut of Claude Opus 4.7, Anthropic's newest flagship. Artificial Analysis published benchmark results placing Opus 4.7 at the top of their Intelligence Index, tied with GPT-5.4 and Gemini 3.1 Pro, while leading the GDPval-AA benchmark for general agentic capability. The index score of 57 represents a 4-point uplift over its predecessor. (<https://xcancel.com/ArtificialAnlys/status/2045292578434875552>) Following up on yesterday's coverage of the Claude Design announcement itself, the benchmarks suggest Opus 4.7 is not merely a vision-model refresh but a genuine capability step. @Alisvolatprop12's side-by-side comparison (344 likes, 30,000 views) was less diplomatic about the competition: running the same prompt through Gemini 3.1 Pro with Deep Think, GPT 5.4 Pro, and Claude Opus 4.7, they found GPT and Opus "produced outputs at a similar level, but Gemini was so utterly terrible" that it appeared to be falling behind. (<https://xcancel.com/Alisvolatprop12/status/2045337052590522724>) On Hacker News, an "Anthropic Nerfed Opus 4.6 Before the 4.7 Launch" thread revived the familiar pattern: users alleging the previous model was degraded ahead of the upgrade to make the new one look better.

The bigger Anthropic story, though, played out in Washington and the Financial Times. Dario Amodei gave an extended FT interview with the headline "I don't want AI turned on our own people" - a direct reference to the Pentagon dispute that has been running since early April, when the Defense Department blacklisted Anthropic. (<https://xcancel.com/FT/status/2045109459605692696>) He discussed Mythos, regulation, and the Pentagon fallout, telling the FT he thinks "we should be thinking about regulating AI the way you regulate cars and aeroplanes." (<https://xcancel.com/FT/status/2045141496798548387>) The interview was the warm-up act for the main event: Axios broke a scoop that Amodei is set to meet White House chief of staff Susie Wiles, described as "a sign of a breakthrough" in the Pentagon dispute. (<https://xcancel.com/axios/status/2045080226418364788>) Yahoo Finance and Reuters both confirmed the meeting. (<https://xcancel.com/YahooFinance/status/2045197223634378766>) (<https://xcancel.com/ReutersBiz/status/2045190552757453098>) Following up on the Pentagon blacklisting covered extensively since 13 April - where the US government simultaneously barred Anthropic from defence work while its most senior officials urgently consulted Amodei about Mythos - this meeting suggests the standoff may be ending. The question is on whose terms.

Amodei also dropped a prediction that drew far more attention than the diplomatic manoeuvring. In remarks attributed by @TFTC21 (774 likes, 771,000 views), he claimed that "50% of all tech jobs, entry-level lawyers, consultants, and finance professionals will be completely wiped out within 1-5 years." (<https://xcancel.com/TFTC21/status/2045250949216895058>) The timeline is notably aggressive even by AI industry standards. Coming from the CEO of the company that built Mythos and just launched Claude Design - a product that visibly threatens an entire category of design work - the statement reads less like speculation and more like a preview.

Over at OpenAI, the news was about departures rather than launches. Kevin Weil, OpenAI's Chief Product Officer, is leaving the company. WIRED broke the story, and ZeroHedge's breaking-news tweet (289 likes, 79,000 views) spread it fast. (<https://xcancel.com/zerohedge/status/2045232738815316288>) Weil ran the "OpenAI for Science" initiative, which is now being "decentralised into other research teams" - corporate language for wound down. In the same wave, Bill Peebles, the creator of Sora, is also departing. Sam Altman's farewell tweet was warm: "really going to miss you, bill! your creativity pushed openai and the world to experience ai video in new ways." (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2045232470917022162>) The context, assembled by @XavierRiveraX (<https://xcancel.com/XavierRiveraX/status/2045305019935637904>), is that OpenAI is actively shutting down "side quests" - Sora reportedly cost a million dollars a day in compute - and refocusing on core enterprise AI. Three senior executives departing in one day is not a normal Thursday. Combined with the Spud launch still pending (the late April to early May window tracked since 9 April remains open), OpenAI appears to be clearing the decks for a concentrated push.

Separately, Altman's World project (formerly Worldcoin) made a significant distribution push. Axios reported that World is partnering with Zoom and Tinder to provide "proof of human" verification, moving from crypto curiosity to mainstream infrastructure. (<https://xcancel.com/axios/status/2045217016999547203>) (<https://xcancel.com/DecryptMedia/status/2045225867777917118>) World Network announced the Zoom integration specifically as anti-deepfake protection - a "Verified Human" badge for video call participants. (<https://xcancel.com/worldnetwork/status/2045198664147365999>) The Verge ran a characteristically arch headline: "Should you stare into Sam Altman's orb before your next date?" (<https://xcancel.com/verge/status/2045242864175833425>) The Tinder partnership is perhaps the most telling. If your dating app needs cryptographic proof that the person you are talking to is human, you have a concise summary of the current moment.

On the research front, Microsoft published MEMENTO, a method that teaches reasoning models to manage their own chain-of-thought context. Rather than letting reasoning grow into a flat 32K-token stream, the model learns to segment its thinking into blocks, compressing and managing them as it goes. @akshay_pachaar's summary (316 likes, 16,000 views) described it as "mass-compressed LLM reasoning." (<https://xcancel.com/akshay_pachaar/status/2045061615935176923>) This is a practical infrastructure advance - reasoning models are expensive precisely because their chains of thought balloon in length, and any technique that compresses that without losing quality has direct cost implications for every provider running reasoning workloads.
