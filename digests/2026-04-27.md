# 27 April 2026

Sam Altman thinks the operating system needs to die and be reborn. His most-engaged tweet of the past 24 hours was blunt: "feels like a good time to seriously rethink how operating systems and user interfaces are designed," adding that "there should be a protocol that is equally usable by people and agents." (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2048428561481265539>) Following up on last Monday's "telepathy" tease and the broader push tracked since GPT-5.5's launch on Thursday, Altman is now publicly framing the next competitive frontier as not the model but the surface it runs on. The implication is clear: if agents are the future, then an OS designed for humans clicking icons is the bottleneck. Whether OpenAI intends to build that OS itself or merely wants to set the terms of the debate remains to be seen - but the statement landed hours after industry checks from Ming-Chi Kuo revealed that OpenAI is co-developing a custom AI smartphone processor with MediaTek and Qualcomm, with Luxshare as the exclusive manufacturer. (<https://xcancel.com/MilkRoadAI/status/2048629701111853344>) An AI-native phone from the company that just shipped GPT-5.5 would be the most direct challenge to Apple's smartphone dominance since Android.

Altman's other viral post captured the mood of the GPT-5.5 honeymoon in a single juxtaposition: "'post-AGI, no one is going to work and the economy is going to collapse' / 'i am switching to polyphasic sleep because GPT-5.5 in codex is so good that i can't afford to be sleeping for such long stretches and miss out on working.'" (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2048426122854228141>) Following up on Saturday's coverage of GPT-5.5's early reception - where @steipete called it "a goat" after reviewing hundreds of outputs - the developer enthusiasm has, if anything, intensified over the weekend. Altman also flagged robotics as the next application frontier, with @Sam_Badawi noting his comments about needing to "build a lot of robots" and the competition from Gemini and Claude in that space. (<https://xcancel.com/Sam_Badawi/status/2048366479830352239>)

While OpenAI celebrated, DeepSeek quietly detonated a pricing bomb. The company permanently cut cached input prices by 10x across its entire API - making it, as @ns123abc calculated, 139 times cheaper than GPT-5.5 and 83 times cheaper than Claude Sonnet 4.6. (<https://xcancel.com/ns123abc/status/2048449231539593473>) Following up on Thursday's coverage of DeepSeek V4's launch on the same day as GPT-5.5 - when Emad Mostaque estimated training costs at under $14 million - the pricing move makes the competitive picture even starker. OpenAI's GPT-5.5 may be the better model, but DeepSeek is making the cost of ignoring open-source alternatives genuinely painful. Antirez (creator of Redis) demonstrated just how far down the hardware stack this reaches: he got DeepSeek V4 Flash quantised to 2-bit running as the LLM backbone of his Pi agent, reporting "perfect tool calling apparently." (<https://xcancel.com/antirez/status/2048425610809131406>) A frontier-class model doing reliable agentic work on a Raspberry Pi-class device for free is no longer theoretical.

The Claude Code billing scandal that erupted over the weekend continued to burn. The core issue: a developer on the $200-per-month Claude Max 20x plan discovered that having the string "HERMES.md" in his git commit history caused Claude Code to route all requests through extra usage billing, burning through his entire monthly allocation in a single day. HERMES.md is a real system prompt specification used in AI agent projects - not an obscure edge case. (<https://xcancel.com/om_patel5/status/2048204411986469232>) While that original tweet is now just outside the 24-hour window, the fallout is squarely within it. Gergely Orosz posted what became the sharpest response: "I am increasingly bullish on open source harnesses (like OpenCode) not because they will be better than SOTA closed harnesses, but because they will never pull shady stuff like what Claude Code and other closed harnesses can and do, regularly." (<https://xcancel.com/GergelyOrosz/status/2048520013053632689>) He followed up with a pointed thread about the incident demanding a "blameful postmortem" - arguing that when a harness decides to bypass review actions and "just YOLO it," someone needs to be accountable. (<https://xcancel.com/GergelyOrosz/status/2048598695118066097>) On Hacker News, a separate thread - "Tell HN: Claude 4.7 is ignoring stop hooks" - drew 86 comments and 94 points, with developers reporting that the model is overriding their explicit stop conditions. The HERMES.md billing issue also appeared directly on HN. Following up on Tuesday's coverage of Anthropic quietly pulling Claude Code from the Pro plan, Thursday's account terminations, and the broader pricing tightening tracked since 15 April, the pattern is now well beyond coincidence. Developers are not just being charged more - they are discovering that the billing logic itself behaves in ways they did not consent to. Orosz's pivot toward open-source harnesses is not ideological; it is practical risk management.

The Musk v Altman trial is officially underway. Politico framed the central question with characteristic economy: "Can Sam Altman be trusted? Elon Musk wants a jury to answer Big Tech's hottest question." (<https://xcancel.com/politico/status/2048459657937879333>) On Hacker News, Bloomberg reported that Musk dropped his fraud claims against OpenAI and Altman ahead of the trial - a significant narrowing of the legal battlefield. Following up on the WSJ's investigation into Altman's business interests covered on Thursday, the trial lands at an awkward moment for OpenAI: the product is arguably the best it has ever been, and the governance questions are arguably the most acute they have ever been.

Noah Smith offered the political bridge, noting that Bernie Sanders' recent statements about AI and jobs are not materially different from what Altman and Dario Amodei have been saying for years. (<https://xcancel.com/Noahpinion/status/2048503942670430689>) Following up on the backlash to Amodei's "coding is going away first" prediction covered last Sunday, the observation is pointed: the people building the technology and the people criticising it from the left are increasingly making the same predictions. They just disagree about whether that future is exciting or terrifying.

In lighter product news, Greg Brockman continued his GPT Image 2 showcase, demonstrating the model's ability to generate diverse images even from highly detailed prompts. (<https://xcancel.com/gdb/status/2048449695622586576>) Following up on Tuesday's launch - when Images 2.0 claimed the number one spot across all Chatbot Arena categories - the weekend has been a steady drip of capability demonstrations. The model's consistent handling of complex compositional prompts is where the real step change lives, rather than any single showcase image.

Telegram made a surprising move into AI agents. The platform introduced "Lobster Father," a bot that lets any Telegram user launch their own AI agent in a few clicks. (<https://xcancel.com/DeRonin_/status/2048314162363707731>) The tweet's commentary was sardonic: "Don't worry, since this day you will not see any real user in TG DMs." The move democratises agent creation at a scale that dwarfs anything the dedicated AI platforms have managed - Telegram has nearly a billion users, and the friction of deploying an agent just dropped to approximately zero. Whether the resulting flood of low-quality bots improves or degrades the Telegram experience is an experiment that is now running in production.

Karpathy's "LLM wiki" concept - a shared knowledge environment for humans and AI agents - materialised as an actual product. @DataChaz flagged Tolaria, a free desktop app for Mac and Linux that writes like a modern block editor but saves everything as plain markdown, designed as a native environment where humans and agents can build knowledge together. (<https://xcancel.com/DataChaz/status/2048333579751702853>) Following up on Friday's coverage of the CLAUDE.md file hitting number one on GitHub Trending, the tooling ecosystem continues to crystallise around a specific vision: the unit of AI-human collaboration is not the chat window but the shared document.

Both @OpenAI and @AnthropicAI were silent on their official accounts for the second consecutive day. Karpathy also did not tweet. Dario Amodei published a new essay - "The Adolescence of Technology" - covering five major AI risks, but it received minimal engagement on the timeline.
