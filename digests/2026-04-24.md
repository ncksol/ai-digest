# 24 April 2026

The Spud watch is over. GPT-5.5 is here.

OpenAI launched GPT-5.5 yesterday evening, describing it as "a new class of intelligence for real work and powering agents, built to understand complex goals, use tools, check its work, and carry more tasks through to completion." (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2047376561205325845>) The model matches GPT-5.4's per-token latency while performing better "across nearly every evaluation," and uses significantly fewer tokens to complete the same Codex tasks - a direct cost reduction for heavy users. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2047376564309115134>) It is rolling out to Plus, Pro, Business, and Enterprise users across ChatGPT and Codex, with a GPT-5.5 Pro tier for the higher plans. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2047376568809636017>) The capability list reads like a product roadmap compressed into a single model: writing and debugging code, web research, data analysis, document creation, software operation, and cross-tool task completion, with particular strength in agentic coding, computer use, and knowledge work. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2047376562916581513>)

Sam Altman's announcement was characteristically understated: "GPT-5.5 is here! We hope it's useful to you. I personally like it." (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2047378253313106112>) His longer thread laid out OpenAI's philosophy - iterative deployment as safety strategy, rapid improvements expected even though the model is already strong. (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2047379615589777666>) Greg Brockman was more direct: "a new class of intelligence" that "completes challenging tasks with little micromanagement." (<https://xcancel.com/gdb/status/2047381612372115812>) Following up on Tuesday's Spud watch - when GPT-5.5 was spotted in both the Codex app and CLI before being pulled back - the launch confirms the late April window that this digest has been tracking since 9 April, when the safety evaluation timeline from the 24 March pretraining completion first pointed here. The codenames (gpt-5.5, oai-2.1, arcanine, glacier-alpha) that @haider1 flagged on Tuesday turned out to be exactly what they looked like: a staged rollout being tested before going public.

Codex got a substantial upgrade alongside the model. The @OpenAIDevs account announced expanded browser use - Codex can now interact with web apps, test flows, click through pages, capture screenshots, and iterate on what it sees. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAIDevs/status/2047381283358355706>) Following up on last Thursday's computer use launch, this extends Codex from a coding tool into something closer to a general-purpose software operator. The Agents SDK also shipped custom approval checks for tool calls, letting developers gate agent actions with their own logic before they run. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAIDevs/status/2047436655863464011>) And Codex Hooks reached general availability - deterministic scripts that fire any time a file is edited, giving developers a layer of programmatic control over AI-generated changes. (<https://xcancel.com/LLMJunky/status/2047539774706061543>) The pattern is clear: OpenAI is building the governance infrastructure around Codex at the same pace as the capabilities. The question of whether enterprises will trust AI agents to operate autonomously is being answered with "no, but here are the guardrails that make supervised autonomy practical."

Altman also revealed a new enterprise play: "We tried a new thing with NVIDIA to roll out Codex across a whole company and it was awesome to see it work. Let us know if you'd like to do it at your company!" (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2047395562501411058>) Following up on yesterday's coverage of OpenAI's workspace agents for enterprise, the NVIDIA partnership is significant - it is one thing to sell individual subscriptions, quite another to deploy an AI coding tool across an entire company's engineering organisation in coordination with the hardware provider. This is the enterprise land grab accelerating.

But GPT-5.5 did not get to enjoy its launch day alone. DeepSeek dropped V4 on the same day, and the open-source community noticed immediately. @TheGeorgePu framed the juxtaposition sharply: "Today. OpenAI shipped GPT-5.5. Today. DeepSeek shipped V4 Flash. Same day. ChatGPT Pro: $200/month. Closed. Rented. DeepSeek V4: free. Fits on a Mac Studio. On coding, it matches Claude Opus. On competition math, it beats GPT-5.4." (<https://xcancel.com/TheGeorgePu/status/2047541015146348571>) ValsAI confirmed the substance behind the hype: DeepSeek V4 is now the number one open-weight model on their Vibe Code Benchmark, beating Kimi K2.6 decisively and even surpassing frontier closed-source models like Gemini 3.1 Pro. (<https://xcancel.com/ValsAI/status/2047513613750202452>) Emad Mostaque estimated the training costs at under $14 million for the Pro variant and under $4 million for Flash - a fraction of what frontier labs spend. (<https://xcancel.com/EMostaque/status/2047545899044213193>) On Hacker News, the DeepSeek V4 Pro paper drew early discussion with the technical community noting its million-token context window. Following up on Monday's coverage of Kimi K2.6 and Ethan Mollick's observation that open-weight models overperform on benchmarks compared to real-world use, the same caveat applies - but the trajectory is undeniable. The gap between open and closed is narrowing faster than the closed labs can widen it.

Replit's Amjad Masad offered the geopolitical framing: "While US politicians/lobbyists are scaremongering about 'Chinese distillation,' Chinese scientists are actually sharing real AI breakthroughs in the open. These kind of advances have nothing to do with data and benefit everyone, including small (and possibly big) US labs." (<https://xcancel.com/amasad/status/2047547275682214384>) The point lands harder on a day when the best open-source model and the most expensive closed model launched simultaneously.

The "Claude was nerfed" narrative, tracked in this digest for weeks, received its most viral validation yet. @levelsio posted: "I can't believe we were right. Claude was dumbified on March 4, just when we noticed!" (<https://xcancel.com/levelsio/status/2047387029915271445>) The tweet pulled 7,100 likes and 675,000 views - a remarkable level of engagement for a complaint about model quality. On Hacker News, a direct link to levelsio's tweet appeared under "Claude Opus 4.6 was nerfed prior to release of Opus 4.7." Following up on the extensive nerfing coverage - from Gergely Orosz's "Claude keeps regressing" on Thursday, to the cache TTL revelation, to the overnight account terminations, to the Pro plan removal covered on Tuesday - this now has a specific date. March 4. Whether it was an intentional degradation to make Opus 4.7's launch look better (as users allege) or an unintended regression (as Anthropic would presumably argue), the community has settled on its verdict.

Anthropic's own news was quieter but substantive. @ClaudeDevs announced that Memory on Claude Managed Agents is now in public beta on the Claude Platform, allowing agents to learn and improve across sessions. (<https://xcancel.com/ClaudeDevs/status/2047424063543681240>) Following up on yesterday's coverage of Anthropic's economic research showing software engineers are the most anxious about AI displacement, the product strategy is clear: make Claude's agents persistent, stateful, and capable of building institutional knowledge. Whether developers trust Anthropic enough to let Claude agents accumulate memory about their workflows - given the pricing instability and account terminations of the past two weeks - is the question.

The software market delivered its own verdict on the AI agent thesis yesterday. ServiceNow fell 18% after posting 22% year-over-year revenue growth - a number that would have been celebrated in any prior era. @danielisdizzy captured the reasoning: "The market thinks LLMs will replace software." (<https://xcancel.com/danielisdizzy/status/2047433320058028338>) On Hacker News, CNBC's coverage of the broader software stock plunge appeared under "Software stocks plunge on ServiceNow, IBM results as AI fears escalate." Following up on yesterday's coverage of the Claude-managed stock portfolio's encounter with a 13% ServiceNow earnings miss, the AI-replaces-SaaS narrative is now actively repricing the entire software sector. ServiceNow's CEO disagrees - arguing that building your own ServiceNow with an LLM would cost ten times more - but the market is not listening.

The WSJ continued its investigation into Sam Altman's business interests, with @Discoplomacy flagging the latest: "Sam Altman is blurring lines between OpenAI and his own business interests. Altman sought OAI investment for two companies he is invested in, one of which was beginning to run short of cash." (<https://xcancel.com/Discoplomacy/status/2047376575386046495>) Following up on last Thursday and Friday's coverage of the WSJ's pre-IPO financial investigation and the ongoing lawsuit from his sister, the timing is notable - the conflicts-of-interest story dropped at the exact moment GPT-5.5 launched, guaranteeing it would be buried. Whether that was coincidence or calendar management is an exercise for the reader.

Earlier in the day, before GPT-5.5 consumed everything, Altman posted two notable tweets. First: "we love seeing our users win. we want to give you the best tools, lots of compute, and watch you do the magic." (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2047347674743963705>) Then, on Images 2.0, launched on Tuesday: "Images 2.0 really got over some important qualitative threshold for me that I didn't know existed." (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2047349336263012771>) Following up on Tuesday's coverage of Images 2.0 claiming the top spot on the Chatbot Arena leaderboard, Altman is now running a multi-front product campaign - GPT-5.5, Images 2.0, Codex computer use, NVIDIA enterprise rollouts - at a pace that suggests OpenAI is trying to make the next 30 days feel like an entire product generation.

Eric Schmidt offered the macro view. As quoted by @rohanpaul_ai: "If you really want to make money, found an agentic AI company. I mean, build an agent to do something. This is the agentic period in AI. Everyone's going to build agents. The agents are all going to compete." (<https://xcancel.com/rohanpaul_ai/status/2047331157931425970>) The former Google CEO's framing aligns neatly with what both OpenAI and Anthropic shipped this week - workspace agents, managed agents with memory, Codex hooks, approval gates. The infrastructure for the "agentic period" is being laid in real time.

A new paper flagged by @dair_ai offered a counterpoint to the agent enthusiasm. Research on diversity collapse in multi-agent LLM systems shows that "multi-agent LLM systems converge on near-identical outputs over time, even across different architectures and different starting prompts." (<https://xcancel.com/dair_ai/status/2047326894992081296>) If all agents think alike, the competitive dynamics Schmidt envisions may produce less variety than a single thoughtful human.
