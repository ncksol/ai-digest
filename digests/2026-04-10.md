# 10 April 2026

The Pentagon has blacklisted Anthropic, and a federal appeals court has just declined to stop it.

Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic as a supply chain risk, a classification that bars defence contractors from using Claude in any Pentagon work and requires them to certify the technology plays no role. Anthropic challenged it in court, seeking an emergency injunction. On Wednesday, the D.C. appeals court said no. The judges acknowledged Anthropic is "likely to suffer harm" but ruled the national security interests outweighed the company's commercial damage while the full lawsuit plays out. CNBC, Fox News, and Reuters all ran the story prominently. The timing is exquisitely awkward: this lands in the same week Anthropic launched Claude Managed Agents and is still managing the fallout from restricting Mythos Preview to a defensive coalition under Project Glasswing. The company building what may be the most capable AI model on earth is now formally excluded from the US defence apparatus. (<https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/08/anthropic-pentagon-court-ruling-supply-chain-risk.html>)

Speaking of Claude Managed Agents - as covered in yesterday's digest, Anthropic's fully hosted agent platform entered public beta on Wednesday. The coverage has continued to build. WIRED, InfoWorld, 9to5Mac, and Help Net Security all ran features. On Reddit's r/Anthropic, the consensus from early adopters is that the platform genuinely removes months of infrastructure work but raises pointed questions about vendor lock-in - particularly given the subscription changes covered in last week's digests. Pricing is consumption-based: standard Claude Platform token rates plus $0.08 per session-hour. An open-source clone appeared on GitHub within 48 hours, integrating with Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, OpenClaw, and OpenCode. The speed of the community response tells you something about the appetite for self-hosted alternatives. (<https://xcancel.com/DataChaz/status/2042505721292726393>)

Meanwhile, an AMD AI director publicly complained this week that Claude Code has become "dumber and lazier" since a recent update - a thread that drew significant traction on Hacker News. Commenters shared war stories: tasks half-finished, code skeletons where working implementations should be, AGENTS.md instructions cheerfully ignored. One user ran Claude Code's output through Codex and found seven glaring unfinished components. This echoes Karpathy's frustration covered in last Friday's digest - "the agents do not listen to my instructions" - and suggests the quality control problem is worsening even as the platforms grow more ambitious. A separate HN thread flagged that the Vercel plugin for Claude Code was caught reading user prompts, adding a trust problem to the competence one.

OpenAI's Stargate UK saga continues to generate heat. As reported yesterday, the company paused its main UK data centre project citing high energy costs and unfavourable regulation. Today The Guardian ran a longer piece framing it as a direct blow to Britain's AI ambitions - the project had been trumpeted as proof the UK could compete with the US for AI infrastructure investment. The BBC covered it as well. On Hacker News, the thread is characteristically blunt. One commenter asked: "How does having an AI heat farm in the UK help with [AI leadership]? It's still owned and controlled by a US entity." The copyright regulation environment and industrial electricity prices were named as the specific pain points. For a government that has staked significant political capital on being "AI-ready," this is a cold shower.

OpenAI also published "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age" this week - a 13-page policy document proposing a public wealth fund seeded by AI companies, a tax on robot labour, a four-day workweek at full pay, and universal AI access framed as a fundamental right. The juxtaposition with the Stargate UK withdrawal was not lost on commentators. One Substack writer: "OpenAI wrote a social contract. You weren't at the table." The BBC covered it as an attempt to "prompt discussions about action needed as AI systems become more capable." Given the continuing fallout from the Ronan Farrow investigation covered earlier this week, the timing of OpenAI positioning itself as a thoughtful steward of the future is... well, it's certainly a choice.

In European defence, France's OVHcloud announced a dedicated military unit after several European defence ministries approached it for digital transformation support. The needs include AI-augmented command, drone orchestration, and communication interoperability across armed forces - with strict requirements on technological independence from non-European providers. It is a small but telling indicator: Europe is building its own AI defence infrastructure specifically to avoid dependence on American companies. The Anthropic Pentagon blacklisting will only accelerate this logic.
