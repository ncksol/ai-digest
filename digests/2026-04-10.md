# 10 April 2026

Anthropic is no longer content to just build the models. Yesterday the company launched Claude Managed Agents - a fully hosted platform for building and deploying AI agents at scale - into public beta. The pitch: define what your agent does, and Anthropic handles servers, security, sandboxing, and orchestration. Pricing is consumption-based: standard Claude Platform token rates plus $0.08 per session-hour for active runtime. TechRadar called it a promise to make agent building "10x faster," while Reddit's r/Anthropic thread was more measured, with early adopters noting the platform removes months of infrastructure work but raises questions about vendor lock-in. The timing is deliberate - this is Anthropic's play to own the agent deployment layer, not just the model layer, arriving hot on the heels of the Mythos Preview announcement covered in Tuesday's digest. (<https://xcancel.com/claudeai/status/2041927687460024721>)

Whether Claude's agents will actually work well is another matter. An AMD AI director publicly complained this week that Claude Code has become "dumber and lazier" since a recent update, a thread that drew considerable sympathy on Hacker News. Commenters shared similar experiences: tasks half-finished, skeletons of code where working implementations should be, instructions in AGENTS.md files cheerfully ignored. One user ran Claude Code's output through Codex and found seven glaring unfinished components. This echoes Karpathy's frustration covered in last Friday's digest - "the agents do not listen to my instructions" - and suggests the quality control problem is getting worse, not better, even as the platforms get more ambitious.

OpenAI, meanwhile, is having quite the week. Reuters broke yesterday that the company is pausing its main UK data centre project - the Stargate UK facility with partner Nscale - citing unfavourable regulation and high energy costs. The blow to the UK government's AI ambitions is significant; the project had been trumpeted as proof that Britain could compete with the US for AI infrastructure investment. On Hacker News, commenters were characteristically blunt: "How does having an AI heat farm in the UK help with [AI leadership]? It's still owned and controlled by a US entity." The copyright regulation environment and industrial electricity prices were named as the specific pain points. CNBC reported that discussions about resuming the project continue, but the signal is clear - the UK is losing the infrastructure race.

The same day, OpenAI published "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age" - a 13-page policy document that reads like a social contract drafted by the company most likely to make that contract necessary. The proposals are eye-catching: a public wealth fund seeded by AI companies, a tax on robot labour, a four-day workweek at full pay, universal access to AI framed as a fundamental right, and containment protocols for autonomous systems. Mother Jones ran a piece headlined "Sam Altman Is Having a Really Weird Week," noting the jarring contrast between these proposals and the continuing fallout from the Ronan Farrow investigation covered in Wednesday's digest. The BBC picked up on it as OpenAI's attempt to "prompt discussions about action needed as AI systems become more capable." One Substack writer was less charitable: "OpenAI wrote a social contract. You weren't at the table."

Following up on Wednesday's coverage of Meta's Muse Spark launch, the reception has settled into a familiar pattern: Wall Street loves it, the technical community is suspicious. Morgan Stanley called it "the first step in re-rating META," while Bank of America compared Meta's trajectory to Alphabet's. But Francois Chollet - creator of Keras and ARC-AGI - was withering, calling Muse Spark "overoptimised for public benchmark numbers at the detriment of everything else." Alexandr Wang, Meta's Superintelligence Labs chief, responded publicly: "We are quite upfront that our model does not perform well on..." - an unusually candid acknowledgement that Muse Spark has blind spots. Simon Willison noted it falls notably behind on Terminal-Bench 2.0. The model is proprietary, a departure from the Llama open-source tradition that Meta says is temporary. Whether that is a strategic pivot or a face-saving measure for a model not yet ready for open scrutiny remains to be seen.

A delightful sidebar from the Meta campus: The Information reported that an employee independently built "Claudeonomics" - an internal leaderboard tracking which of Meta's 85,000+ employees consumed the most AI tokens. Achievement titles included "Model Connoisseur" and "Cache Wizard." The total: over 60 trillion tokens in a recent 30-day period. Zuckerberg did not crack the top 250. Meta promptly killed the dashboard after internal data was leaked publicly, leaving the identity of the "Token Legend" forever unknown. One suspects the leaderboard will live on in spirit if not in official infrastructure.

Shopify made a quieter but potentially more consequential move. The company released a 16-skill AI Toolkit giving any AI coding agent - Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot, Gemini CLI - direct write access to the entire Shopify store backend: products, orders, inventory, SEO, images. As @aakashgupta noted, this is $378 billion in GMV and 5.6 million stores now accessible to autonomous agents. The implications for e-commerce automation are enormous, and for e-commerce security, somewhat terrifying. (<https://xcancel.com/aakashgupta/status/2042415274511704434>)

The GPT-6 countdown continues to tick. As reported in Wednesday's digest, the model codenamed "Spud" completed pretraining around 24 March at the Stargate data centre in Abilene, Texas. Polymarket still gives it 78% odds of releasing by month end. OpenAI is retiring GPT-5.2-Codex and GPT-5.1-Codex-Mini from Codex on 14 April - six days from now - which increasingly looks like the clearing-of-the-decks pattern that precedes a major launch. Multiple sources now converge on 16 April as the likely date. If GPT-6 lands next week while Anthropic is still gatekeeping Mythos to a defensive coalition and Meta is arguing about benchmarks, the competitive dynamics shift dramatically.

**Also notable:**

- The Guardian reported that Palantir engineers working on the NHS Federated Data Platform have been issued NHS.net email accounts, giving them access to a staff directory of up to 1.5 million employees. NHS staff have raised concerns about the access level.
- Two PhD students built Terminator-1, an AI agent they claim beats Claude Mythos at 95%+ on SWE-bench Verified and Terminal-Bench - which, if verified, would be a remarkable result for an academic project competing against a 10-trillion-parameter model.
- Bessemer published a deep dive on Shopify's AI-first engineering playbook, revealing they built an internal LLM proxy that routes all AI requests through a single gateway rather than mandating one tool - a pattern other large engineering organisations would do well to study.
