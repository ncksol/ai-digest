# 7 April 2026

Anthropic is going big on silicon. The company announced it has signed an agreement with Google and Broadcom for "multiple gigawatts" of next-generation TPU capacity, coming online from 2027, to train and serve frontier Claude models. The phrasing is deliberately vague on specifics, but the scale is striking - as one Hacker News commenter pointed out, a single nuclear reactor delivers roughly 1 GW, so "multiple gigawatts" implies data centre capacity that would make a small nation's grid operator nervous. The HN thread quickly became a discussion about how we even measure compute at this scale, with one commenter noting that "measuring data centres in watts is like measuring cars in horsepower - power isn't a direct measure of performance, but of the primary constraint on performance." The deal also signals Anthropic's deepening reliance on Google's infrastructure even as the two companies compete directly in the model market. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2041275561704931636>)

Over at OpenAI, a notable departure: Joanne Jang, the woman who effectively invented model behaviour tuning and shaped GPT-4o's personality, announced she is leaving after four and a half years. For those who don't follow the org chart, Jang was one of the key figures determining how OpenAI's models actually talk - their tone, their guardrails, their character. Her departure is the latest in a steady trickle of senior talent leaving the company. (<https://xcancel.com/ns123abc/status/2041270419013918931>)

In an entirely different register, one of the more unexpected things to land on the timeline this week: actor Milla Jovovich and a collaborator called bensig have open-sourced MemPalace, an AI memory system that uses a "palace" architecture - wings, halls, rooms - mirroring how the memory palace technique works for humans. The bold claims: 100% recall on the LongMemEval benchmark (a first, they say), 92.9% on ConvoMem (more than double Mem0's score), and 100% on LoCoMo. It runs entirely locally, no API key, no cloud, MIT-licensed. The compression trick - "AAAK compression" - reportedly fits an entire life context into 120 tokens. Given how much attention Karpathy's local knowledge base work drew over the weekend (as covered in Friday through Sunday's digests), the timing here is interesting - clearly there is an appetite for local-first, user-controlled memory systems that don't depend on any vendor. (<https://xcancel.com/bensig/status/2041236952998171118>)

A research paper making the rounds deserves attention for what it says about LLM limitations. @aakashgupta broke down the SenseMath study, which tested five models across 4,800 maths problems. The finding isn't simply that LLMs struggle with arithmetic - it's more specific and more damaging. When explicitly told "use this shortcut," accuracy jumped up to 15%. The models could execute the technique fine. But left to their own devices, they used shortcuts less than 40% of the time, even when they had already demonstrated they knew how. Worse still: when asked to judge whether a shortcut applied, the models said yes to nearly everything, including problems where the shortcut gives the wrong answer. As aakashgupta put it: "The gap between 'can execute' and 'can recognise' is where most enterprise AI deployments are quietly failing." (<https://xcancel.com/aakashgupta/status/2041410154189730025>)
