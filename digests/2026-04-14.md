# 14 April 2026

Stanford HAI dropped its annual AI Index Report yesterday, and the headline finding lands like a cold shower on Washington: China has erased the US lead in AI. The two countries now trade places at the top of performance benchmarks with metronomic regularity. The US still leads on capital and chip infrastructure, but China has pulled ahead on patents, publications, and physical AI (autonomous robotics). South Korea, meanwhile, has emerged as the world's leader in innovation density, filing more patents per capita than any other nation. The report, covered by IEEE Spectrum, MIT Technology Review, Nature, The Register, KQED, and Fox News, paints a picture of a technology sprinting ahead of society's capacity to govern it. Responsible AI is "not keeping pace with AI capability," the researchers write, with safety benchmarks lagging and incidents rising sharply. Public trust in governments to regulate AI has hit new lows - just 31% of US citizens express confidence, the lowest of all surveyed nations except China at 27%. More than 90% of notable AI models are now built by private companies, and those companies have largely stopped disclosing dataset sizes and training durations. On Hacker News, the thread focused on the growing disconnect between AI insiders and the general public, with commenters noting that software job openings have been roughly flat for years despite breathless predictions of an AI hiring boom.

The Altman saga has escalated beyond the metaphorical. Following Friday's Molotov cocktail attack on his San Francisco home by a 20-year-old arrested near OpenAI's headquarters, Altman's residence was struck by gunfire early Sunday morning. San Francisco police arrested two individuals - Amanda Tom, 25, and Muhamad Tarik Hussein, 23 - and seized three firearms. It is unclear whether the shooting was connected to the firebombing or deliberate at all. NBC News reported that the Molotov suspect had expressed anti-AI views in his writings. As covered in Wednesday's digest, the Ronan Farrow investigation in The New Yorker had already placed Altman at the centre of an intense public debate. His blog post on Friday - sharing a family photo of his husband and child, asking people to "de-escalate the rhetoric and tactics" - reads differently now that a second attack has followed. TechCrunch and the LA Times both framed the post as a response to the New Yorker piece as much as to the firebombing. Whatever one thinks of Altman or OpenAI, two attacks on a CEO's home in 48 hours marks an alarming escalation in anti-AI sentiment.

Beneath the violence, the corporate machinery grinds on. A leaked internal memo from OpenAI's Chief Revenue Officer Denise Dresser, obtained by The Verge, lays out the company's Q2 strategy with unusual candour - and takes direct aim at Anthropic. Dresser claims Anthropic is "grossing up" revenue-sharing agreements with Google and Amazon to inflate its reported run rate, overstating it by roughly $8 billion (which would drop Anthropic from $30 billion to $22 billion, conveniently below OpenAI's $24 billion). She describes Anthropic as "built on fear, restriction, and the idea that a small group of elites should control AI." Gizmodo's headline captured the vibe: "OpenAI Exec Reveals New Strategy in Leaked Memo: Attack Anthropic." The memo also references the Spud model, calling it "an important step in the intelligence foundation for the next generation of work." Following up on the Spud tracking throughout this past week, the memo confirms early customer previews are underway. Altimeter CEO Brad Gerstner, speaking on the All-In Podcast, said people using Spud are reporting it is "on par with Mythos" and called this moment "peak OpenAI FUD." No official launch date has been confirmed, though last week's digest noted 16 April as the date to watch. The Dresser memo also reveals OpenAI is building an agent platform called "Frontier" positioned as "the default platform for enterprise agents," and describes a "Stateful Runtime Environment" on AWS that goes beyond model access to enable memory and context continuity.

Anthropic, for its part, is dealing with a trust problem of its own making. The Register reported that Anthropic quietly reduced the Claude Code prompt cache TTL from one hour to five minutes around early March, and users have been burning through quotas at alarming rates ever since. A developer named Sean Swanson filed a detailed bug report showing the change, and Jarred Sumner (creator of Bun, now at Anthropic) acknowledged it was "good detective work" but argued the shorter TTL actually makes Claude Code cheaper for one-shot calls. Swanson's response: he has been a $200-per-month subscriber for six months and never hit a quota limit until March. On Reddit's r/ClaudeAI, users parsed 119,866 API calls and confirmed the TTL shifted between late February and early March. A community fix - claude-code-cache-fix - forces the one-hour TTL and reportedly achieves 98.4% cache hit rates. On Hacker News, a thread titled "Anthropic downgraded cache TTL on March 6th" drew pointed commentary about eroding trust, with one commenter noting: "Every time I hear Anthropic mentioned in the past few weeks, it's almost always in some negative context." As covered extensively in Thursday and Saturday's digests, the "Claude has been nerfed" narrative has gone from vibes to documented evidence. The cache TTL revelation is another data point in the same direction.

In a distinctly different register, the Washington Post reported that Anthropic hosted a closed-door summit with fifteen Catholic and Protestant leaders in late March to discuss Claude's "moral and spiritual development." The questions included how to handle users who are grieving or at risk of self-harm, and what attitude the chatbot should adopt toward its own potential demise - being shut off. Premier Christian News, Heise, India Today, and the Christian Post all covered it. One participant reportedly came away convinced that Anthropic is "genuinely wrestling" with the questions rather than performing concern. Whether an AI company consulting theologians about its chatbot's soul represents progress or parody probably depends on your priors, but as a data point about how seriously Anthropic takes alignment beyond the technical, it is at least novel.

Today is also the day OpenAI retires GPT-5.2-Codex and GPT-5.1-Codex-Mini from Codex, as noted in yesterday's digest. The clearing pattern ahead of the Spud release is now complete.
