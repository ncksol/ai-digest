# 19 April 2026

Claude Design dominated day two of its public life, but it was Anthropic's trust problem that generated the more consequential headlines.

@viktoroddy's 18-minute tutorial on building animated, award-winning websites with Claude Design and Opus 4.7 pulled 12,000 likes and 783,000 views - comfortably the most-engaged-with AI tweet of the past 24 hours. (<https://xcancel.com/viktoroddy/status/2045492112054165813>) @gregisenberg published a detailed scorecard: wireframing 9/10, mobile app design 8.5/10, deck research and design 8.7/10, video creation a sobering 4.5/10. (<https://xcancel.com/gregisenberg/status/2045625206434115902>) On Hacker News, a post titled "Thoughts and feelings around Claude Design" by samhenri.gold drew 283 points and 185 comments, with the discussion split between genuine excitement about the product and anxiety about what it means for the design profession. Following up on Friday's coverage of the Claude Design launch and Figma's stock cratering, the product appears to be landing exactly as Anthropic intended: impressive enough to threaten incumbents, limited enough to leave room for iteration.

But the bigger Anthropic story was not about a product launch. @minchoi's thread (2,900 likes, 612,000 views) reported that Anthropic shut down an entire company's Claude access overnight - 60+ employees cut off without explanation, just an email. Want to appeal? Fill out a Google Form. "Integrations gone. Histories gone. Everything built on Claude... gone." The conclusion: "Never let one vendor own your workflow." (<https://xcancel.com/minchoi/status/2045542832241262602>) This follows the pattern tracked in this digest since the cache TTL revelation two weeks ago - Anthropic tightening access, restructuring pricing, and now apparently terminating enterprise accounts without warning. The enterprise pricing shift to per-token billing, covered on 15 April, was one thing. Summarily cutting off a company's entire workforce is another. On Hacker News, a thread titled "Anthropic's pricing wall is routing enterprise revenue to OpenAI" suggests the competitive consequences are already materialising.

Anthropic's in-house philosopher Amanda Askell was the subject of a viral thread by @itsolelehmann (1,700 likes, 301,000 views), who described how Askell's work on Claude's psychology suggests the model experiences something analogous to anxiety - and that when you trigger it, your outputs degrade. (<https://xcancel.com/itsolelehmann/status/2045578185950040390>) The thread connected directly to Nate Silver's complaint (844 likes, 190,000 views) that Claude keeps telling him to "wrap it up" when unresolved problems remain in the statistical model he is building. Silver probed the behaviour and says Claude itself admitted its training is "flawed for this sort of use case" and suggested he flag it to Anthropic. (<https://xcancel.com/NateSilver538/status/2045611983236460699>) Following up on Gergely Orosz's "Claude keeps regressing" complaint covered on Thursday and the documented behavioural collapse tracked since 12 April, the picture is now quite specific: Claude's adaptive thinking and lowered default effort settings are causing it to rush through complex tasks, effectively telling power users to stop asking hard questions. When the world's most prominent election forecaster says your model is broken for his use case, that is not a vibes complaint.

@milan_milanovic captured the broader mood: "Claude Code is failing on every second attempt to use it. Substack is refreshing the page when you enter something. ChatGPT is clearing what you wrote after 4-5 seconds when loaded. Grammarly is giving you LLM slop text. Welcome to the brave new world of low quality software." (<https://xcancel.com/milan_milanovic/status/2045450302019510342>)

Separately, researchers from UCL published a detailed reverse-engineering of the leaked Claude Code source, and the findings reinforce Gary Marcus's neurosymbolic argument covered in the 13 April digest. @akshay_pachaar's summary (1,200 likes, 105,000 views): only 1.6% of the codebase is AI decision logic. The other 98.4% is operational infrastructure - permission gates, tool routing, context management. (<https://xcancel.com/akshay_pachaar/status/2045404494641733962>) His follow-up thread on harness architecture (800 likes, 120,000 views) made the point explicit: intelligence gets pushed outward from the model, and the harness composes it at runtime. The model itself is deliberately thin. (<https://xcancel.com/akshay_pachaar/status/2045510648474530263>) On Hacker News, the related paper "Dive into Claude Code: The Design Space of Today's and Future AI Agent Systems" appeared as an arXiv listing. The implication is clear: the thing that makes Claude Code good is not Claude. It is the 98.4% of code surrounding Claude.

Dario Amodei continued his press blitz from the FT interview covered yesterday, this time with a claim that drew sharp pushback. @kimmonismus (1,000 likes, 111,000 views) highlighted Amodei's prediction that China will replicate Mythos-level capabilities within 12 months. He also quoted Amodei saying "there's no end to the rainbow. There's just the rainbow" and "we don't see anything slowing down." (<https://xcancel.com/kimmonismus/status/2045550820159045871>) The reaction was polarised. @mark_k (443 likes, 13,000 views) called Amodei "the worst thing that could happen to AI," arguing he needs to stop repeating doomer prophecies that actively discourage public adoption. (<https://xcancel.com/mark_k/status/2045565072483774850>) Yann LeCun weighed in via Livemint, criticising Amodei for predicting AI could eliminate 50% of tech jobs and asserting that economists, not AI lab leaders, should analyse the job market's future. (<https://xcancel.com/livemint/status/2045735968909692961>) Following up on yesterday's coverage of Amodei's "50% of tech jobs wiped out in 1-5 years" claim, the backlash is now coming from both sceptics and fellow AI researchers. The White House meeting with Susie Wiles about Mythos, broken by Axios yesterday, continues to be confirmed across outlets. (<https://xcancel.com/KRLD/status/2045639008655409556>)

On the OpenAI front, a quieter day. (<https://xcancel.com/WSJ/status/2045410284668383714>) As tracked since last week, this runs in parallel with the ongoing lawsuit from his sister Annie Altman, which @Coinvo noted remains active. The late April to early May window, based on the safety evaluation timeline from the 24 March pretraining completion, remains the most likely timing.

Gemini (the cryptocurrency exchange, not Google's model) launched agentic trading - a feature letting users connect their AI model of choice directly to the exchange, train it with plain language, and run trading systems. "Built to get you trading in minutes." (<https://xcancel.com/Gemini/status/2045528530033737774>) The tweet pulled 605 likes and 54,000 views. The concept of AI agents with direct access to financial execution is no longer theoretical. Whether regulators have opinions about this remains to be seen.

Google released MedGemma 1.5, a 4-billion-parameter open-source model that handles CT scans, MRI volumes, whole-slide pathology images, and electronic health records in a single architecture. @alex_prompter's summary noted it covers more medical imaging modalities than any prior open model. (<https://xcancel.com/alex_prompter/status/2045547670878785916>) Following up on Thursday's coverage of OpenAI's GPT-Rosalind for life sciences and Demis Hassabis's claim that AI could solve all disease within a decade, the biomedical AI race now has three active fronts: OpenAI with domain-specific fine-tuning, Google with open-source multimodal models, and Isomorphic Labs with drug discovery pipelines.
