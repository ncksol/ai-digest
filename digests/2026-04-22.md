# 22 April 2026

SpaceX dropped the biggest AI partnership announcement of the year. SpaceXAI and Cursor are "now working closely together to create the world's best coding and knowledge work AI," combining Cursor's product and developer distribution with SpaceX's Colossus training supercomputer - a million H100-equivalent cluster. (<https://xcancel.com/SpaceX/status/2046713419978453374>) The announcement, which pulled 6 million views in under nine hours, immediately redraws the competitive map for AI coding tools. Cursor was already the fastest-growing AI code editor; pairing it with what is reportedly the largest single training cluster in the world gives it a compute moat that neither Codex nor Claude Code can match through their respective parent companies alone. Following up on last Thursday's coverage of OpenAI launching Codex computer use and Altman personally campaigning for developers to switch from Claude Code, the coding tool wars just gained a third well-resourced combatant with its own silicon. On Hacker News, The Guardian's coverage of the deal drew early discussion about whether this signals Cursor pivoting from inference-only to training its own models.

OpenAI, meanwhile, had its own big day. ChatGPT Images 2.0 launched - described as "a state-of-the-art image model that can take on complex visual tasks and produce precise, immediately usable visuals, with sharper editing, richer layouts, and thinking-level intelligence." (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2046670977145372771>) This is OpenAI's first image model with integrated thinking capabilities: when a reasoning model is selected, Images 2.0 can search the web for reference, generate multiple distinct images from a single prompt, and self-verify its outputs. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2046670989719924768>) It also handles non-English text rendering with actual coherence rather than the garbled approximations that have plagued every prior model. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2046670983092990411>) The teaser that preceded the launch - OpenAI simply posting "This is not a screenshot" alongside an AI-generated image indistinguishable from one - pulled 3.8 million views. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2046589828918317155>) The Chatbot Arena leaderboard confirmed the substance behind the marketing: GPT-Image-2 claimed the number one spot across all Image Arena categories, with a record-breaking 242-point lead in text-to-image generation. (<https://xcancel.com/arena/status/2046670703311884548>) On Hacker News, the ChatGPT Images 2.0 thread hit 713 points and 551 comments, making it one of the most active AI discussions this month.

Sam Altman was characteristically busy. He teased the launch at breakfast - "Really excited for this week! Next up, we've got something to show you at 12 pm PT today" (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2046598595869331894>) - then celebrated with a manga of himself and @gabeeegoooh hunting for GPUs, generated by the new model. (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2046672912833458597>) His post-launch message was blunt: "We want you to have a lot of AI!" (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2046752492093165708>)

But Altman's most consequential statement of the day was not about images. TechCrunch reported that he dismissed Anthropic's Mythos cybersecurity model as "fear-based marketing." (<https://xcancel.com/TechCrunch/status/2046664599697830039>) @kyliebytes noted that "sam altman is not subtle about what he thinks of anthropic's mythos." (<https://xcancel.com/kyliebytes/status/2046582332136820800>) Following up on the extensive Mythos coverage tracked since 8 April - the Pentagon blacklist, the NSA using it anyway, the failed Amodei-Wiles meeting, the Polymarket odds collapsing to 19% as covered yesterday - Altman is now openly attacking the Mythos narrative itself. The framing is strategic: if Mythos is "fear-based marketing" rather than a genuine capability breakthrough, then OpenAI's GPT-5.4-Cyber (launched on 15 April as a defensive tool for vetted researchers) looks measured by comparison. Whether the characterisation is fair is another matter entirely - the zero-day discoveries were independently verified.

Anthropic's pricing squeeze continued to tighten. @TheGeorgePu flagged that Anthropic has quietly pulled Claude Code from the Pro plan, requiring users to upgrade to Max at $100 per month minimum - a fivefold jump. "No announcement. Just a pricing page edit." (<https://xcancel.com/TheGeorgePu/status/2046705634331025855>) The tweet pulled 2.8 million views, making it one of the most-engaged-with AI tweets of the day. Following up on the enterprise pricing restructure covered on 15 April (per-token billing replacing flat subscriptions), the overnight account terminations covered on 19 April, and the ID verification controversy on 16 April, this is now the fourth distinct pricing or access tightening in under two weeks. The pattern is unmistakable: Anthropic is systematically monetising its developer base while simultaneously losing government revenue (the Pentagon blacklist) and burning through the goodwill that made developers choose Claude in the first place. The SpaceX-Cursor deal landing on the same day is particularly poor timing for Anthropic - developers being priced out of Claude Code now have somewhere obvious to go.

The Spud watch may be nearing its conclusion. @LLMJunky reported that "GPT 5.5 has been spotted in the Codex App. Release imminent." (<https://xcancel.com/LLMJunky/status/2046812561383866678>) Hours later, @haider1 provided corroboration: "openai is testing different models with codenames found in the codex CLI as well as in the app: gpt-5.5, oai-2.1, arcanine, glacier-alpha, glacier-alpha-block-cy3/cy4. Now they seem to have pulled back all the models." (<https://xcancel.com/haider1/status/2046838944537411676>) This is the most concrete evidence yet that Spud - tracked in this digest since 9 April, when the safety evaluation window from the 24 March pretraining completion first pointed to late April - is real and nearly ready. Multiple codenames in both the app and CLI, followed by a pull-back, is consistent with a staged rollout being tested before public launch. The late April window is now looking very tight indeed.

Replit's Amjad Masad announced the Security Agent - "a new approach that merges static analysis with AI reasoning" to address the wave of security vulnerabilities in AI-generated code. (<https://xcancel.com/amasad/status/2046662176661024995>) Following up on Sunday's coverage of the Lovable data breach and the broader pattern of AI-first platforms shipping without adequate security, this is the first major product response to the problem. The timing is pointed: if AI tools are generating insecure code at scale, then AI tools need to be finding and fixing the vulnerabilities too.

Nous Research had a productive 24 hours, making StepFun's Step 3.5 Flash available free for ten days on the Nous Portal (<https://xcancel.com/NousResearch/status/2046811525118132557>) and adding native GPT Image 2.0 support to Hermes Agent. (<https://xcancel.com/NousResearch/status/2046693872773062834>) @RoundtableSpace captured the broader trend: "A free private AI agent on your laptop is starting to look way more real," describing how Hermes, Ollama, and Gemma 4 combine into a local agent with web research and self-improving skills at zero monthly cost. (<https://xcancel.com/RoundtableSpace/status/2046691693936935232>)
