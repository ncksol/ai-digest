# 5 April 2026

Karpathy's latest viral moment has nothing to do with a new model or a startup. Instead, it's about markdown files.

In a post that racked up 46,000 likes and nearly 14 million views, the former Tesla AI chief laid out his workflow for building personal knowledge bases using LLMs and Obsidian. The idea is deceptively simple: dump raw sources (papers, articles, repos, datasets) into a folder, have an LLM compile them into an interlinked markdown wiki, then query that wiki for complex research questions. No fancy RAG pipeline needed - the LLM maintains its own indexes. Outputs like slides, charts, and markdown files get filed back into the wiki, so every question makes the system smarter. He even vibe-coded a search engine over his own wiki. (<https://xcancel.com/karpathy/status/2039805659525644595>)

The post went so viral that Karpathy followed up with what he calls an "idea file" - a deliberately abstract gist you feed to your own agent so it can build a customised version for you, rather than sharing a specific repo. "There is less of a point/need of sharing the specific code/app, you just share the idea, then the other person's agent customizes & builds it," he wrote. (<https://xcancel.com/karpathy/status/2040470801506541998>)

On Hacker News, the idea file post hit the front page with 225 points and lively discussion. Several commenters shared similar setups but flagged a critical failure mode: there's a complexity threshold beyond which the agent can't keep the wiki consistent and the human can't grok it anymore. One commenter called it "a weird new type of tech debt - almost like a persistent brain gap." (<https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47640875>)

The other big OpenAI news: ChatGPT is now available in CarPlay, rolling out to iPhone users on iOS 26.4+. Voice mode in the car - for better or worse. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2039748699350532097>)

Meanwhile, Anthropic made a move that's annoyed a fair chunk of its power users. Starting yesterday (4 April), Claude subscription limits no longer cover third-party harnesses like OpenClaw. You can still use them, but they now draw from "extra usage" - a pay-as-you-go option billed separately. Anthropic is offering a one-time credit (up to $200 on Team plans) and discounts on pre-purchased usage bundles, but the message is clear: capacity is finite, and they're prioritising their own products. On HN, the thread drew sharp commentary, with users noting this effectively makes Claude Code and Claude Cowork the only first-class citizens of your subscription. (<https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47633568>)

One practical tip surfaced amid the grumbling: @LLMJunky shared a Claude Code configuration trick - setting `compactThreshold` to 200K, using Haiku for subagents, and switching to `/effort medium` - that apparently cuts usage rates substantially. The punchline of the thread, naturally, was "step 5: install Codex." (<https://xcancel.com/LLMJunky/status/2040127436864586166>)

In research, Ethan Mollick highlighted "Mr. Chatterbox" - an LLM trained entirely from scratch on 28,000 Victorian-era British texts from the British Library (1837-1899). Unlike a modern LLM roleplaying a Victorian, this one genuinely doesn't know what comes next - no hindsight contamination. The HN thread was enthusiastic, with commenters dreaming of similar models for other historical periods and noting the potential for digitising vast archival holdings at the National Archives in Kew. (<https://xcancel.com/emollick/status/2038084424810537215>) (<https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47582056>)
