# 20 April 2026

The Vercel breach dominated the weekend, but it was the NSA's quiet hypocrisy on Mythos that carried the sharper edge.

Vercel confirmed that its internal systems were compromised, with attackers claiming to be selling internal database access, employee accounts, and GitHub/NPM tokens for $2 million on BreachForums. @shiri_shh's breaking tweet linked the breach to "early access to Claude Mythos" - a claim that remains unverified but perfectly timed to stoke the Mythos panic cycle. (<https://xcancel.com/shiri_shh/status/2045883344324640817>) @paraschopra connected the dots more soberly: "Vercel got hacked. Axios too. Many other companies will get hacked too. The weak link is often a human falling for phishing attack. This year we will see a massive rise in these data breaches, thanks to AI capabilities both in social engineering and weakness discovery." (<https://xcancel.com/paraschopra/status/2046090279523148271>) On Hacker News, the Vercel breach thread drew 393 comments and became the weekend's most active security discussion, with developers debating whether to rotate every secret that ever touched Vercel's infrastructure.

The connection to Mythos - whether real or speculative - lands differently now. Polymarket broke the news that the NSA has reportedly been using Claude Mythos despite Anthropic's "supply chain risk" designation by the Pentagon. (<https://xcancel.com/Polymarket/status/2045959036059746721>) Following up on Friday's coverage of Dario Amodei's scheduled meeting with White House chief of staff Susie Wiles, the picture is now actively absurd: the Pentagon blacklisted Anthropic, the NSA kept using Mythos anyway, and Amodei walked into the White House to discuss the situation - only for Trump to reportedly respond "Who?" when Amodei's name was mentioned. (<https://xcancel.com/Clubic/status/2046097345423540607>) The Hacker News thread on the NSA report was small but pointed, with commenters noting the obvious: if even the intelligence community cannot resist using Mythos, the "supply chain risk" designation is theatre. As tracked since the Pentagon blacklisting on 13 April, the US government's AI posture is now openly incoherent - different agencies taking opposite positions on the same model from the same company in the same month.

Dario Amodei continued to generate headlines, though increasingly of the kind he probably did not intend. @Coinvo surfaced a new quote: "Coding is going away first, then all of software engineering." (<https://xcancel.com/Coinvo/status/2045812320014594369>) Following up on the "50% of tech jobs wiped out" prediction covered in Friday and Saturday's digests, the backlash has now crossed into parody. @trajektoriePL's mock tweet - "Marathoners are cooked. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei: 50% of all athletic jobs and sports professionals will be wiped out within 1-5 years" - pulled 7,700 likes and half a million views, making it comfortably the most-engaged-with Amodei-related content of the weekend. (<https://xcancel.com/trajektoriePL/status/2045785699023028339>) When the parodies outperform the original statements by an order of magnitude, the message is not landing as intended.

@The_AI_Investor offered the most consequential framing of Anthropic's current position: "Sam Altman may have been right to book a ridiculous amount of compute in advance, as we've now seen what happened to Anthropic: they simply don't have enough compute to serve their models." (<https://xcancel.com/The_AI_Investor/status/2045980230066790761>) Following up on 15 April's coverage of Anthropic's enterprise pricing restructure - the shift from flat subscriptions to per-token billing driven by a compute crunch - this is the supply-side consequence of building the world's most capable model without the infrastructure to run it at scale. OpenAI's massive compute pre-purchases, long mocked as extravagant, now look like foresight.

The Claude "emotion" debate from yesterday's digest continued to run hot. Gary Marcus was characteristically blunt: "Let's get one thing straight. Claude doesn't get anxious. It mimics people who get anxious. Those two things are NOT the same. My head is shaking so much I need medical attention." (<https://xcancel.com/GaryMarcus/status/2045986607010824405>) He followed up with a crisper formulation: "Claude's 'emotion' vectors are 'emotionlike' in roughly the same sense that counterfeit 'money' is 'moneylike.'" (<https://xcancel.com/GaryMarcus/status/2046060295508914185>) Following up on Saturday's coverage of Amanda Askell's work on Claude's psychology and Nate Silver's complaint about Claude rushing through complex tasks, Marcus is making the philosophical counter-argument: whatever the internal representations look like, calling them emotions is a category error that misleads both users and developers.

Demis Hassabis proposed what may be the cleanest test for AGI anyone has offered. As summarised by @rohanpaul_ai: "Train a model on all human knowledge but cut it off at 1911, then see if it can independently discover general relativity (as Einstein did by 1915). If yes, it's AGI." (<https://xcancel.com/rohanpaul_ai/status/2045912351690592708>) The elegance is in the specificity - not "can it pass an exam" or "can it code" but "can it make a genuinely novel scientific discovery from first principles, given only what was known at a specific historical moment?" It is the kind of test that renders most benchmark debates trivial by comparison.

Ethan Mollick flagged two notable observations. First, the continuing gap between Gemini Pro 3.1's capabilities as a model and the Gemini app's abilities as a product: "The model can do what Claude/GPT can do, but there is a minimal harness for tools, no auditable chain-of-thought/actions, manual [intervention needed]." (<https://xcancel.com/emollick/status/2045909435315323321>) Following up on Wednesday's coverage of Google's coordinated Mac launch and YouTube Premium bundle, this is the persistent Google problem: excellent models, mediocre product wrapping. The harness engineering gap, documented in this digest since the Claude Code architecture analysis on 19 April, applies to Google more than anyone. Separately, Mollick reflected on what he called "the second most important release of the LLM era (after GPT-3.5), featuring what was likely the most important chart," noting his surprise that "OpenAI told everyone about the biggest advance in AI technology since the LLM rather than keeping it to themselves." (<https://xcancel.com/emollick/status/2046053467941163055>)

On the Spud watch: @kimmonismus's reaction to early reports was simply "what the heck. GPT-5.5 pro will be a qualitative leap. Absolutely insane." (<https://xcancel.com/kimmonismus/status/2045838993426719051>) No official announcement, no model card, no blog post - but the early-access reactions are now leaking consistently, and they are uniformly positive. The late April to early May window remains the most likely timing. Both OpenAI and Anthropic were silent on their official accounts over the weekend.

Francois Chollet - creator of Keras and the ARC benchmark - posted one of the more thoughtful observations of the week: "Human cognitive friction has long been acting as a regulariser for a lot of digital infrastructure. It made software APIs less terrible and codebases less complex. Now LLM disintermediation is causing this effect to fade, which in turn will cause runaway software complexity." (<https://xcancel.com/fchollet/status/2045929951539707957>) The argument is counterintuitive but compelling: the difficulty of writing and reading code served as a natural quality filter. Now that AI can produce code faster than humans can review it, the constraint that kept systems manageable is dissolving. The result will not be less code but more - and worse.

Andrej Karpathy weighed in on a related phenomenon: "All the Enneagram type fives get one-shotted into AI psychosis when they discover LLM knowledge bases. And an especially strong fit when you have a lot of pre-existing data to insert into it." (<https://xcancel.com/karpathy/status/2046017433199374610>) The observation connects to a new research paper flagged by @ihtesham2005 introducing the concept of the "LLM Fallacy" - the cognitive error where "individuals misinterpret LLM-assisted outputs as evidence of their own independent competence." (<https://xcancel.com/ihtesham2005/status/2045825382281269627>) Between Karpathy's AI psychosis and the LLM Fallacy, the emerging picture is of a technology that systematically distorts its users' self-assessment - making them feel more capable than they are while subtly degrading their ability to notice.

On the infrastructure front, @jukan05 flagged that Chinese AI chip companies including Huawei and Iluvatar are now testing self-produced HBM3 integrated with their own AI processors. (<https://xcancel.com/jukan05/status/2046094621953155310>) Following up on the Stanford HAI report covered on 14 April - which documented China erasing the US lead on AI patents and publications - the hardware gap is now closing too. And on Hacker News, a developer posted about receiving a EUR 54,000 billing spike in 13 hours from an unrestricted Firebase browser key accessing Gemini APIs - a cautionary tale that drew 288 comments and 398 points about what happens when AI API access is left unguarded.

@aakashgupta highlighted a Google DeepMind paper arguing that the entire AI agent threat model is wrong: "Everyone is securing the model. Jailbreak defences. Prompt injection filters. Alignment training. The attack surface is the internet." The paper catalogues how websites can already detect when an AI agent visits and serve it completely different content from what humans see. (<https://xcancel.com/aakashgupta/status/2045905440484266447>)
