# 21 April 2026

Anthropic solved its compute problem - or at least threw a staggering amount of money at it. The company announced an expanded collaboration with Amazon to secure up to 5 gigawatts of compute for training and deploying Claude, with capacity beginning to come online this quarter and nearly 1 gigawatt expected by the end of 2026. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2046327624092487688>) Amazon is investing an additional $5 billion in Anthropic immediately, with up to $20 billion more in the future. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2046327625367625773>) Following up on Sunday's coverage of @The_AI_Investor's observation that Anthropic "simply don't have enough compute to serve their models" - and the broader narrative tracked since the enterprise pricing restructure on 15 April, where per-token billing replaced flat subscriptions because of a compute crunch - this deal is a direct answer. Five gigawatts is an extraordinary figure; for context, that is roughly the output of five large nuclear power stations. The bet is clear: Anthropic is wagering that the demand for Mythos-class inference will justify infrastructure at an industrial scale. Whether the enterprise customers who were blindsided by the pricing shift will stick around long enough to use it remains the open question. On Hacker News, an early thread on the announcement drew discussion about AWS's growing concentration risk as the infrastructure backbone for both its own AI ambitions and Anthropic's.

The Pentagon dispute, however, continues to deteriorate. Polymarket reported that odds on the Anthropic Mythos government deal fell 13 percentage points to just 19% after Dario Amodei's White House meeting with Susie Wiles ended without a breakthrough. (<https://xcancel.com/PolyNewsHub/status/2046462205550330295>) Following up on Sunday's coverage of the NSA reportedly using Mythos despite the Pentagon blacklist, and Friday's reporting of the Wiles meeting itself, the picture is now actively bleak for Anthropic's government prospects. The blacklist remains in place. The meeting produced nothing. The Polymarket odds - which had been above 30% - suggest the market now views resolution as unlikely. Anthropic is simultaneously securing billions in private compute capacity and watching its government business evaporate. Alongside the Amazon deal, Anthropic launched the STEM Fellows Program, inviting experts in science and engineering to work alongside its research teams on specific projects. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2046362119755727256>) It reads as Anthropic doubling down on private-sector and research applications while the defence door stays shut.

The biggest security story of the day came not from frontier models but from a vibe-coding platform. @weezerOSINT disclosed a mass data breach at Lovable - the AI app builder - affecting every project created before November 2025. A newly created free account could access other users' source code, database credentials, AI chat histories, and customer data. (<https://xcancel.com/weezerOSINT/status/2046170666131669027>) The tweet pulled 5,200 likes and 1.2 million views inside 24 hours. Coming a day after the Vercel breach dominated Sunday's digest, this is the second major security incident in the AI development toolchain in 48 hours. The pattern is familiar: fast-growing AI-first platforms prioritise shipping over security, and the consequences arrive in bulk. The difference with Lovable is the scope of what was exposed - not just tokens and credentials, but the entire project state of every early user, including their customers' data.

(<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2046330082726384051>) The pairing - an effusive Apple tribute followed by a cryptic product tease - is consistent with the pattern tracked since Thursday's Codex computer use launch: Altman is personally campaigning across multiple fronts simultaneously, whether pitching Codex, praising potential partners, or stoking anticipation for whatever "telepathy" turns out to be. Moonshot AI's Kimi K2.6 was the open-source story of the day. @aakashgupta's thread laid out the case: the model matched or beat Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, and Gemini 3.1 Pro on six benchmarks, dropped the weights on HuggingFace, and the Western AI press barely covered it. (<https://xcancel.com/aakashgupta/status/2046283765543317789>) Ollama moved quickly, making K2.6 available on its cloud with integrations for OpenClaw and Hermes Agent. (<https://xcancel.com/ollama/status/2046361868076487155>) Ethan Mollick offered the counterpoint that matters: "I find that open weights models over-perform on benchmarks compared to actual real-world usage, and Kimi feels like no exception. A small amount of use will show that Kimi is not as good as Claude Opus 4.6, which it beats on the benchmarks. Still a good model, tho!" (<https://xcancel.com/emollick/status/2046430301593751797>) On Hacker News, the Kimi K2.6 thread drew 336 comments and 642 points - one of the most active open-source model discussions this month - with the community split between enthusiasm for frontier-tier open weights and scepticism about benchmark gaming. The benchmark-reality gap that Mollick describes is becoming a recurring theme: models look competitive on paper, but the experience of actually using them tells a different story.

This is particularly significant against the backdrop of what @blip_tm flagged as a structural shift: Meta is reportedly moving toward closed-source models, which would be a devastating blow to AI chip startups whose entire strategy depends on serving open-weight models like Llama. "The Groq/Cerebras strategy of just serving Llama until you find a big customer doesn't work without a model to serve, and DeepSeek is the only frontier open one left." (<https://xcancel.com/blip_tm/status/2046343131696685494>) If Meta goes closed and Kimi K2.6 is the best open alternative, the open-source AI ecosystem's dependence on a single Chinese lab becomes a strategic vulnerability that nobody seems ready to discuss.

The Steve Yegge vs Google drama, covered in Thursday's digest, continued to reverberate. Gergely Orosz weighed in: "A few days ago, Steve posted about how AI usage is low at Google, in good part because Gemini is just not as good as e.g. Claude Code, and Claude is not allowed across most of Google. Google pushed back: but it seems to be true! Bad optics for Google..." (<https://xcancel.com/GergelyOrosz/status/2046345353473950163>) Following up on Hassabis's unusually sharp public rebuttal - "stop spreading absolute nonsense" - the fact that one of the most widely-read software engineering commentators is now confirming Yegge's account makes the denial harder to sustain. The core issue, as flagged in Wednesday's coverage of Mollick's observation about the Gemini app's minimal harness for tools, remains unchanged: Google has excellent models and mediocre internal tooling around them.

@HowToAI_ posted a thread arguing that "Yann LeCun was right the entire time. And generative AI might be a dead end." The thread, which pulled 7,400 likes and 563,000 views, revisited LeCun's long-standing critique that scaling LLMs alone will not produce general intelligence. (<https://xcancel.com/HowToAI_/status/2046254937559237012>) The timing is notable: this lands in the same week that benchmark-beating open models are being described as underwhelming in real use, and in the same month that every major lab has pivoted from model scaling to harness engineering as the driver of practical capability. Whether LeCun is right about the dead end or merely right that scaling alone is insufficient is the distinction that matters - and the industry's actions suggest the latter, even as the discourse leans toward the former.

@akshay_pachaar continued his excellent series on AI agent security with coverage of a new Google DeepMind paper mapping "AI Agent Traps" - the first systematic framework for adversarial content engineered to hijack AI agents browsing the web. (<https://xcancel.com/akshay_pachaar/status/2046151867177308181>) Following up on Sunday's coverage of the same DeepMind paper (flagged by @aakashgupta), which catalogued how websites can detect AI agents and serve them completely different content, the research is now getting the detailed treatment it deserves. The threat model is simple and alarming: the attack surface for AI agents is not the model. It is the internet.

@steipete offered a sharp observation on the economics of AI subscriptions: "These highly subsidised subs are out there to get your code to improve their models. If you use AI for things useful to you, but not code, you are not valuable to them." (<https://xcancel.com/steipete/status/2046199257430888878>) The framing recontextualises the entire AI subscription market - the below-cost pricing is not philanthropy; it is a data acquisition strategy where the product you are building with AI tools becomes the training data for the next generation.
