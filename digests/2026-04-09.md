# 9 April 2026

Meta has finally shown what $14.3 billion buys you. Muse Spark, the first model from Meta Superintelligence Labs - the unit led by former Scale AI chief Alexandr Wang - launched yesterday, and the reaction has been a mixture of cautious optimism and institutional memory. The model, originally code-named Avocado, was built from scratch over nine months and is natively multimodal (voice, text, and image input, text output at launch). Meta is not positioning it as a frontier beater but rather as "small and fast by design, yet capable enough to reason through complex questions in science, math, and health." A "Contemplating" mode, which uses parallel agent reasoning, is rolling out gradually and is pitched as competitive with Gemini Deep Think and GPT Pro for hard queries.

On Hacker News, the thread drew the scepticism you would expect from anyone who remembers Llama 4's disappointing launch last April. One commenter flagged an NYT report from March revealing that Avocado had initially fallen short of Gemini 3.0 on internal tests, and that Meta had discussed temporarily licensing Gemini to power its products. Others pushed back: if Muse Spark even matches Opus 4.6, it proves Meta can build competitive models in-house, which matters enormously for a company running AI across Instagram, WhatsApp, and VR at scale. The model is proprietary - a notable departure from Meta's open-source Llama strategy, though they say they "hope to open-source future versions." Meta's stock jumped 6.5% on the day, though that was likely more about Trump suspending Iran strikes than AI benchmarks.

The bigger strategic picture: Meta's 2026 AI capex is now projected at $115-135 billion, nearly double last year. OpenAI and Anthropic are collectively valued above $1 trillion. The generative AI market is expected to grow 40% annually to $325 billion by 2033. Meta's bet is that it cannot afford to keep renting intelligence from rivals - it needs its own.

Meanwhile, the countdown to OpenAI's next move is getting louder. A model code-named "Spud" has leaked with an apparent release date of April 16 - one week from today. The evidence: pretraining reportedly wrapped around 24 March, the model is in safety evaluation, Polymarket gives it 78% odds of dropping by month end, and OpenAI is retiring GPT-5.2-Codex and GPT-5.1-Codex-Mini from Codex on April 14 - a classic pre-launch clearing pattern. As reported in Monday's digest, hints had already surfaced that OpenAI was preparing something Mythos-class. Now the specifics are firming up: if Spud scores in the high 70s on SWE-bench Pro (Mythos Preview sits at 77.8%), it will likely be called GPT-6. If it lands in the low 60s, expect GPT-5.5. Greg Brockman reportedly described it as representing "two years of research" with a "big model feel" - not the language of an incremental release. The expected rollout is ChatGPT Plus and Pro first, free tier two to four weeks later, then enterprise API.

This would make April 2026 the month three major labs each dropped a generational model. Following up on this week's coverage of Claude Mythos Preview, Anthropic's decision not to release its most capable model publicly continues to generate mainstream coverage. The New York Times ran an opinion piece calling Anthropic's restraint "a terrifying warning sign," while NBC, Forbes, Gizmodo, and Inc all ran features. The consensus among commentators: whether you view the restricted release as responsible stewardship or a marketing masterstroke, the capability gap Mythos represents is real and the industry is scrambling to close it.

The Altman situation has taken a turn for the worse. A major investigation by Ronan Farrow and Andrew Marantz dropped in The New Yorker on Monday, based on interviews with more than 100 people and previously undisclosed documents. The allegations centre on a pattern of manipulation and lack of transparency - telling different versions of events to different people, offering the same job to two candidates simultaneously, and lying about safety protocols. Ilya Sutskever is quoted, and the piece frames AI as "as much a power story as a technology story." Bloomberg's opinion desk followed up with a blunt column: even though the IPO documents are unlikely to mention it, "one of the biggest risk factors facing the company is Altman himself." Separately, Elon Musk filed to have Altman removed as CEO and board member as part of his ongoing legal challenge to OpenAI's for-profit conversion, asking any trial winnings to go to OpenAI's nonprofit.

On a more constructive note from OpenAI: the company published a Child Safety Blueprint, developed with the National Centre for Missing & Exploited Children and the Attorney General Alliance. The framework focuses on three pillars - modernising legislation to cover AI-generated abuse material, improving reporting mechanisms to law enforcement, and building preventative safeguards directly into AI systems. The Verge, TechCrunch, and others covered it as a significant policy intervention, though the timing alongside the Farrow investigation is... convenient.

**Also notable:**

- CrowdStrike announced AI security innovations at RSAC 2026, integrating Seraphic technology to monitor autonomous agent activity in browsers - positioning endpoint protection as the new battleground for AI governance.
- Sam Altman, in lighter news, told a podcast it will take "another year" before ChatGPT can start a timer. Humans have been keeping time since 3500 BC. The models are still figuring it out.
