# 23 April 2026

OpenAI launched workspace agents in ChatGPT - shared agents that can handle complex tasks and long-running workflows across tools and teams. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2047008987665809771>) The feature, available in research preview for Business, Enterprise, Edu, and Teachers plans, lets users describe a job and have ChatGPT build a working agent that can coordinate across docs, email, chats, code, and external systems like Linear and Slack. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2047008990379512047>) (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2047008991944069624>) Sam Altman's endorsement was characteristically casual: "These are cool! I think most companies will want to use them." (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2047017964105597009>) On Hacker News, the announcement drew early discussion, with the thread on the official blog post accumulating substantive comments about how this compares to existing workflow automation tools. The positioning is significant: OpenAI is no longer selling a chatbot. It is selling autonomous workers that live inside your company's tool stack. Following up on last Thursday's Codex computer use launch and the broader push to make OpenAI the default enterprise AI platform, workspace agents represent the logical next step - from an assistant you talk to, to an employee you delegate to.

Google Cloud Next brought a parallel announcement. Google introduced the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, describing the conversation around AI agents as "no longer about how to build them - it's about how to manage thousands of them." (<https://xcancel.com/Google/status/2046985650868547851>) The platform combines models, services, and governance tools for building, scaling, and optimising agents at enterprise scale. Google also announced new features for Gemini Enterprise itself, including a new inbox for managing, monitoring, and acting on agent outputs. (<https://xcancel.com/Google/status/2046988686433108417>) The timing is pointed: OpenAI and Google launching enterprise agent platforms on the same day makes this less an announcement and more a land grab. The question is no longer whether enterprises will deploy AI agents but whose agents they will deploy - and whose governance layer they will trust.

Anthropic had a different kind of day. The company published new research based on what 81,000 people told them they want from AI, this time focused on economic hopes and fears. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2047006548149289017>) The headline finding: people in both the highest- and lowest-paid occupations report the largest productivity gains from AI, but those with the biggest speedups also have the greatest concern about job displacement. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2047006550859125228>) Software engineers - heavy Claude users - were notably more worried about displacement than those in lower-exposure roles. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2047006554403057776>) Anthropic is launching a monthly Anthropic Economic Index Survey to track these trends over time. (<https://xcancel.com/AnthropicAI/status/2047006557183897664>) Following up on Dario Amodei's repeated predictions that 50% of tech jobs will be automated within 1-5 years - tracked in this digest since 18 April - this is Anthropic publishing data that validates the anxiety its own CEO has been stoking. Whether the intention is responsible transparency or a form of managed expectation-setting is a matter of interpretation.

Claude itself had a productive 24 hours. Interactive charts and diagrams arrived in Claude Cowork, available in beta on all paid plans. (<https://xcancel.com/claudeai/status/2047047633416397076>) And the @ClaudeDevs account published a substantial blog post on building agents that reach production systems with MCP - covering when agents should use direct APIs versus CLIs versus MCP, plus patterns for building MCP servers and context-efficient clients. (<https://xcancel.com/ClaudeDevs/status/2047086372666921217>) Meanwhile, Dylan Patel of SemiAnalysis revealed that Claude Code spend at his firm had reached a $10.95 million annual run rate before Opus 4.7 brought it back from the brink. "More token efficient for tasks, smarter, and no fast mode. Thank you Anthropic. You saved me from bankruptcy." (<https://xcancel.com/dylan522p/status/2047104466512400639>) Following up on yesterday's coverage of Anthropic quietly pulling Claude Code from the Pro plan and pushing users to the $100/month Max tier, the juxtaposition is striking: the power users who spend the most are simultaneously the ones being priced out and the ones singing Opus 4.7's praises. The model got better. The business model got worse. Both things are true.

The viral thread of the day belonged to @dhruvtwt_, who flagged that Nvidia is offering roughly 80 AI models via hosted APIs completely free - including MiniMax M2.7, GLM 5.1, Kimi 2.5, DeepSeek 3.2, and GPT-OSS-120B, all plugging into coding tools and agent frameworks. (<https://xcancel.com/dhruvtwt_/status/2047006444701274380>) The strategic logic is straightforward: Nvidia wants developers building on models that run on Nvidia hardware. Free API access is customer acquisition for the GPU business. But for individual developers, the practical result is the same - frontier-class inference at zero cost.

OpenAI extended its medical AI push with ChatGPT for Clinicians, offered free to credentialed healthcare professionals, alongside HealthBench Professional for benchmarking LLM performance on medical tasks. (<https://xcancel.com/EricTopol/status/2047126414554701836>) Following up on last Thursday's GPT-Rosalind launch for life sciences, OpenAI now has dedicated medical products at both the research tier (Rosalind) and the clinical tier (ChatGPT for Clinicians). The three-way biomedical AI race between OpenAI, Google's MedGemma, and Isomorphic Labs continues to accelerate.

Satya Nadella announced Microsoft's largest investment in Australia to date - A$25 billion committed to expanding AI and cloud capacity, strengthening cybersecurity, and building digital skills. (<https://xcancel.com/satyanadella/status/2047118960941023552>) The infrastructure buildout continues globally, with every major player racing to secure compute capacity in new geographies.

Sam Altman's World project hit an embarrassing snag. WIRED reported that World (formerly Worldcoin) had claimed Bruno Mars would use its verification technology for upcoming tour ticket sales - but a spokesperson for Bruno Mars and Live Nation told WIRED the partnership never existed and they were never even approached. (<https://xcancel.com/ZeffMax/status/2047079513859961195>) Following up on last Friday's coverage of World partnering with Zoom and Tinder for "proof of human" verification, this is a significant credibility hit for a project whose entire value proposition rests on trust.

The Economist published a provocative editorial framing the AI future as controlled "with almost godlike command" by five men: Dario Amodei, Demis Hassabis, Elon Musk, Mark Zuckerberg, and Sam Altman, then asking which is the most dangerous. (<https://xcancel.com/TheEconomist/status/2046891841270743430>)

Following up on Monday's coverage of Kimi K2.6's open-source release and the benchmark debate it sparked, Moonshot AI announced K2.6 Agent Swarm - a dramatic scaling of agent capabilities to 300 parallel sub-agents and 4,000 steps per run, up from 100 and 1,500 respectively in K2.5. Outputs are delivered as real files rather than chat - a single run can produce 100+ files, 100,000-word literature reviews, or 20,000-row datasets. (<https://xcancel.com/Kimi_Moonshot/status/2047190578493096122>) Whether K2.6's agent capabilities match the benchmarks better than its raw model performance matched real-world usage (as Ethan Mollick questioned on Monday) remains to be seen. But the sheer scale of parallel agent coordination is a statement of intent.

@stocktalkweekly flagged an interesting pattern: the "deep research" modes of Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini are all spending dramatically less time "thinking" over the past month. "An anecdote for constraint in inference compute?" (<https://xcancel.com/stocktalkweekly/status/2047086744890232958>) Following up on the ongoing coverage of Anthropic's compute crunch and the pricing squeeze that followed, the observation raises a question that applies to all three providers: are reasoning models being quietly throttled to manage costs?

Patrick McKenzie offered a more positive data point, describing how he sent Opus 4.7 to do a literature survey of government law enforcement internal guidelines on AML and KYC - and found it "much more successful at finding these docs than I would have expected." (<https://xcancel.com/patio11/status/2046999846838485481>) When someone as careful as patio11 is impressed, it is worth noting.

A new Nature paper, flagged by Eric Topol, proposed that LLM hallucinations should be addressed by rewarding models for admitting uncertainty rather than confidently guessing. (<https://xcancel.com/EricTopol/status/2047120672523190383>) The obvious counter: every user who has ever typed "are you sure?" and received an equally confident but different answer already knew this.
