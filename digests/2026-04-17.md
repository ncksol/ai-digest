# 17 April 2026

OpenAI had a double-header yesterday, and both announcements landed hard. The bigger of the two: Codex got computer use. The desktop app can now see, click, and type on your Mac using its own cursor, running in the background without hijacking your screen. It supports 90+ plugins, an in-app browser, learns from previous actions, and can handle ongoing and repeatable tasks. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2044827705406062670>) (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2044827932145897652>) (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2044828378147311990>) Sam Altman was unusually effusive, calling computer use "even more useful than I expected" and noting it can run apps in parallel without interfering with your direct work. (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2044858862042591378>) He followed up hours later with a pointed joke aimed at Tibo - "I am happy everyone is switching to Codex, but if you start rate limiting me or making me use worse models..." - which pulled 6,300 likes and 888,000 views. (<https://xcancel.com/sama/status/2044921348540264614>) The subtext is clear: OpenAI wants Codex, not Claude Code, to be the default AI coding tool, and Altman is personally campaigning for the switch. Following yesterday's coverage of Gergely Orosz's frustrations with Claude's regressions (more on which below), the timing is sharp.

The second OpenAI launch: GPT-Rosalind, a frontier reasoning model purpose-built for life sciences. It is optimised for protein and chemical reasoning, genomics analysis, biochemistry, and scientific tool use, positioned squarely at drug discovery and translational medicine. OpenAI framed it around the 10-15 year drug approval timeline in the US and the potential for AI to compress it. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2044861690911850863>) (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2044861694216900901>) (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2044861692555985207>) Axios ran the story. (<https://xcancel.com/axios/status/2044857050874679513>) On Hacker News, the GPT-Rosalind thread drew 22 comments and 88 points, with discussion centring on whether a domain-specific model represents genuine progress or a marketing repackaging of fine-tuning. The naming is a nice touch - Rosalind Franklin, whose X-ray crystallography was essential to discovering DNA's structure, finally getting her due in a model name rather than a footnote.

This is notable alongside Demis Hassabis's claim, surfaced in an interview with Max Jaderberg (president of Isomorphic Labs), that AI could solve all disease within a decade. (@peterottsjo's thread drew 196 likes and 23,000 views.) (<https://xcancel.com/peterottsjo/status/2044873497197887757>) OpenAI and DeepMind are now explicitly competing on the same biomedical AI narrative, with Rosalind as OpenAI's first dedicated entry and Isomorphic as Google's.

Meanwhile, Anthropic launched @ClaudeDevs - a dedicated developer-facing account on X. The announcement tweet pulled a staggering 18,000 likes and 6.1 million views, making it the single most-engaged-with AI tweet of the past 24 hours. (<https://xcancel.com/ClaudeDevs/status/2044780198722498580>) "For the developers building with Claude, a direct line from the team" - changelogs, API releases, community updates. The launch reads as a direct response to the trust erosion covered extensively in recent digests. After weeks of cache TTL reductions, subscription restructuring, and the ID verification controversy, Anthropic is trying to rebuild the developer relationship through transparency and direct communication. Whether it works depends entirely on what the account actually does versus what it promises.

The need is acute. Gergely Orosz - The Pragmatic Engineer, one of the most widely-read voices in software engineering - posted a pointed complaint about Claude's continued regression. "Claude just keeps regressing for me, day after day," he wrote, noting that web search functionality that worked days ago now simply refuses to fire. "It's like showing you the middle finger." The tweet pulled 1,700 likes and 151,000 views. (<https://xcancel.com/GergelyOrosz/status/2044814640471810346>) Following up on the "Claude has been nerfed" narrative tracked since the cache TTL revelation two weeks ago, this is no longer a vibes-based complaint from a few power users. It is a sustained, documented pattern that Anthropic's new @ClaudeDevs account will need to address with more than changelogs.

(<https://xcancel.com/FT/status/2044998120287736230>) Following up on Saturday's coverage of UK regulators holding urgent talks about Mythos-class capabilities and Monday's coverage of Jamie Dimon's warning, the alarm has now gone fully global. This is the Mythos story's third week, and it has moved from Anthropic's controlled disclosure to regulatory panic to front-page FT.

MiniMax and Nous Research announced a collaboration bringing M2.7 together with Hermes Agent, describing it as a "co-evolution between models and harnesses." Nous's self-improving loop is designed to bring out the best in M2.7 through real usage. (<https://xcancel.com/MiniMax_AI/status/2044745282785886469>) The tweet pulled 1,000 likes and 215,000 views. Following up on Sunday's coverage of M2.7's open-source release and its novel self-evolving agent architecture, the Nous partnership positions MiniMax's model as the open-source agent backbone - exactly the niche that matters most as harness engineering overtakes raw model weights in determining agent performance. @akshay_pachaar's viral thread on the evolution from "weights to context to harness engineering" (771 likes, 102,000 views) captured this shift perfectly. (<https://xcancel.com/akshay_pachaar/status/2044763357300027842>)
