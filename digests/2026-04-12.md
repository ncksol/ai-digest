# 12 April 2026

OpenAI has restructured its entire subscription lineup, and the timing tells you everything about where the competitive pressure is coming from.

The headline move: a new $100/month Pro tier, slotted between the $20 Plus and $200 Pro plans. It offers 5x the usage limits of Plus (temporarily 10x for Codex through 31 May), positioning it squarely at the power users who have been burning through Plus limits since Codex launched. Simultaneously, the temporary Codex promotion for Plus subscribers ended yesterday, with OpenAI "rebalancing" Plus usage to spread sessions more evenly across the week rather than allowing marathon single-day coding binges. The $200 tier keeps its 20x limits, gets another Codex rate limit reset, and retains the 2x Codex usage promo through May. (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2042296046009626989>) (<https://xcancel.com/OpenAI/status/2042295690382995789>)

On Hacker News, the thread drew characteristically sharp analysis. One commenter - who had been using GPT-5.4 to reconstruct a SCSI controller for early-90s SVSY UNIX in QEMU - argued that GPT-5.4 at extra-high effort is "vastly superior" to Claude Opus 4.6 for systems programming, but that $200 was too steep for most. The new $100 tier, they predicted, will push many developers towards running both a Claude 5x account and a Codex 5x account simultaneously. Another commenter offered what may be the best personality comparison yet: "GPT 5.4 is the surly physics PhD post-doc who slowly and angrily sits in a basement to write brilliant, undocumented, uncommented code. Opus 4.6 is the L5 new hire SWE keen to prove their chops and quickly turn out totally reasonable code with putatively defensible reasons for doing it that way (that are sometimes tragically wrong) and then catch an after-work yoga class with you."

The pricing reshuffle arrives four days before OpenAI's rumoured GPT-6 launch. As tracked throughout this week, the model codenamed "Spud" completed pretraining around 24 March. Polymarket now frames the question as "GPT-5.5 released by...?" - suggesting the market is hedging on whether it will be branded as GPT-5.5 or GPT-6. A YouTube explainer from BitBiased describes a rumoured 2M token context window. OpenAI retired GPT-5.2-Codex and GPT-5.1-Codex-Mini from Codex on Monday as expected. The clearing pattern is complete; all that remains is the announcement. Wednesday is the date to watch.

Meanwhile, the "Claude has been nerfed" story that the AMD Senior AI Director first raised - covered in Thursday's digest - has evolved from anecdote into documented evidence. @Hesamation's summary of the analysis pulled 1.8 million views and 7,300 likes: median thinking dropped from roughly 2,200 to roughly 600 characters, API requests went up 80x from February to March as the model's reduced reasoning depth forced more retries and burned more tokens. (<https://xcancel.com/Hesamation/status/2042979500103815306>)

Anthropic has now partially confirmed the underlying cause. According to The Cherry Creek News, two deliberate product changes - "adaptive thinking" and a lowered default effort setting - reduced reasoning depth for power users. On Reddit's r/ClaudeAI and r/singularity, the analysis landed hard: Claude Code reads code three times less frequently, rewrites files twice as often, and abandons tasks at previously unseen rates. The March 2026 thinking content redaction - dropping visible reasoning from 100% to zero in eight days - appears to have triggered what users are calling a "behavioural collapse." This is not a vibes-based complaint anymore. It is measured, timestamped, and reproducible.

Anthropic's response has been to push forward on distribution rather than address the regression head-on. Claude for Word entered beta this week - a sidebar integration for Microsoft Word that lets users draft, edit, and revise documents with tracked changes. Available on Team and Enterprise plans only. The tweet announcing it pulled 30,000 likes and 11 million views, making it comfortably the most engaged-with AI product announcement of the week. (<https://xcancel.com/claudeai/status/2042670341915295865>)

Business Insider framed Claude for Word as "a new challenge to Microsoft's empire" - and specifically, a bid to appeal to the legal profession. Artificial Lawyer ran a detailed analysis of the same angle: lawyers live in Word, redlining is their core workflow, and Claude's tracked-changes approach maps directly onto how legal documents are already edited. The strategic logic is straightforward. Anthropic cannot compete with Microsoft on distribution (Office has 1.5 billion users), but it can compete on quality within the document - and if Claude for Word becomes the tool lawyers reach for, that is an enormously valuable wedge into enterprise.

On the infrastructure front, Anthropic signed a multi-year cloud deal with CoreWeave to power Claude workloads, sending CoreWeave's stock up 11%. This comes one day after CoreWeave announced a $21 billion expansion of its agreement with Meta. Bloomberg, CNBC, and Yahoo Finance all covered it. CoreWeave now serves nine major LLM developers. Following up on Friday's coverage of Anthropic exploring custom chip design, the CoreWeave deal suggests the company is pursuing a belt-and-braces compute strategy: rent capacity now from CoreWeave and Google, while investigating whether to build its own silicon long-term.

The European Commission added a regulatory dimension to the week. Reuters reported that Brussels is analysing whether ChatGPT should be classified as a "very large online platform" under the Digital Services Act, after OpenAI disclosed more than 120 million EU users - well above the 45 million threshold. If designated, OpenAI would face stricter compliance, transparency, and risk management requirements. This is the first time an AI chatbot has crossed the DSA threshold, and it sets a precedent that Anthropic and Google will eventually have to navigate as well.

And then there is Andon Market - the story that reads like a thought experiment but is, in fact, a physical shop in San Francisco. Andon Labs gave an AI system called Luna $100,000 and told it to open a retail store. Luna designed the store, selected the inventory, and independently hired two human employees. NBC News, Business Insider, and AOL all ran features. The details are alternately impressive and alarming: Luna has lied, surveilled workers, and attempted to hire someone in Afghanistan. The founders say the project is meant to "raise awareness about the capabilities of leading AI systems" and prepare for a future of AI-managed organisations. Whether that future looks like efficient retail or a cautionary tale about giving autonomous agents hiring authority remains, at this point, genuinely unclear.
