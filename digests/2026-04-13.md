# 13 April 2026

The Mythos story has reached the people who actually control financial infrastructure, and they are not calm about it.

The Financial Times reported yesterday that British financial regulators - the Bank of England, the Financial Conduct Authority, and the Treasury - are holding urgent talks with the National Cyber Security Centre and major banks to assess the risks posed by Claude Mythos Preview. Leading banks, insurers, and exchanges are being warned about cybersecurity vulnerabilities the model has exposed. This is no longer a tech industry conversation. As covered extensively in last week's digests, Mythos found zero-day vulnerabilities in every major operating system and browser, built working exploits autonomously, and prompted Anthropic to assemble Project Glasswing rather than release the model publicly. Now the regulatory apparatus of one of the world's largest financial centres is scrambling to determine whether its infrastructure is at risk from the capabilities Mythos represents - and, more pointedly, from whatever comes after it.

The timing of the UK response makes more sense when you consider what CNBC revealed on Thursday: Vice President JD Vance and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent personally questioned leading tech CEOs about AI security in a call that took place just before Anthropic released Mythos. The call included Sam Altman, Sundar Pichai, and Dario Amodei. Bessent separately summoned bank CEOs for what AOL described as an "emergency meeting" on the same subject. Following up on Wednesday's coverage of the Pentagon blacklisting Anthropic, this creates a remarkable picture: the US government simultaneously bars Anthropic from defence work while its most senior officials urgently consult Anthropic's CEO about the security implications of its model. The left hand and the right hand are not merely uncoordinated - they appear to be operating in different universes.

Not everyone is buying the narrative. Yann LeCun, Meta's chief AI scientist, publicly dismissed the entire Mythos saga over the weekend. "Mythos drama = BS from self-delusion," he wrote on X, characterising the panic as overblown theatrics. Gary Marcus weighed in from a similar angle, writing that "to a certain degree, I feel that we were played." The Guardian ran a long analysis piece headlined "Too powerful for the public: inside Anthropic's bid to win the AI publicity war," questioning whether Mythos is genuinely a breakthrough or a masterclass in controlled disclosure. The sceptics' argument is straightforward: Anthropic built a model, wrapped it in dramatic framing, assembled a coalition of trillion-dollar companies, briefed the White House, and generated weeks of breathless coverage - all without the public being able to verify any of the claims independently. It is either responsible stewardship or the most effective marketing campaign in AI history. Possibly both.

Meanwhile, at the HumanX conference in San Francisco, 6,500 executives, founders, and investors gathered last week to talk about AI - and according to CNBC, TechCrunch, and multiple other outlets, the conversation was overwhelmingly about Claude, not ChatGPT. TechCrunch headlined it bluntly: "At the HumanX conference, everyone was talking about Claude." CNBC's framing was sharper: "OpenAI no longer dominates the conversation." Developers specifically cited Claude's coding performance. Enterprise buyers discussed Managed Agents and Claude for Word. The shift in industry sentiment - from OpenAI-first to Anthropic-first - appears to have accelerated dramatically in the past fortnight, driven by the combined force of Mythos, Claude Code's developer loyalty, and Anthropic's aggressive enterprise push. Whether OpenAI can reclaim the narrative depends substantially on what happens in three days.

About those three days. As tracked throughout the past week, today is the day OpenAI retires GPT-5.2-Codex and GPT-5.1-Codex-Mini from Codex - the final clearing pattern before the rumoured 16 April launch of the model codenamed "Spud." The Polymarket question is now framed as "GPT-5.5 released by...?" and the odds remain strong for an end-of-April release. A BitBiased explainer describes a rumoured 2M token context window. Whether it lands as GPT-5.5 or GPT-6 depends on benchmark performance - if it scores in the high 70s on SWE-bench Pro (Mythos sits at 77.8%), expect the bigger number. Wednesday is the date to watch.

OpenAI has a more immediate problem to manage first. On Friday, the company disclosed a supply chain attack: a North Korea-linked group compromised the third-party Axios library used in OpenAI's build process, forcing the company to rotate its macOS application signing certificates and urge all desktop users to update. OpenAI says no user data or API keys were accessed, and the root cause was a misconfigured GitHub Actions workflow that has since been fixed. The irony of OpenAI suffering a supply chain attack in the same week that Anthropic's model is demonstrating superhuman vulnerability-finding capabilities writes itself.

Gary Marcus, the AI researcher and persistent industry critic, chose this particular moment to publish what might be his most concession-laden piece yet. In a Substack post titled "The biggest advance in AI since the LLM," Marcus argues that Claude Code is genuinely impressive - but not for the reasons most people think. The Claude Code source leak, which Anthropic tried and failed to suppress via DMCA takedown, revealed a 3,167-line kernel called print.ts at the system's core. It is a pattern-matching engine - symbolic AI, not neural. Marcus's conclusion: Claude Code works as well as it does because Anthropic figured out that for the parts that really matter, you cannot trust a pure LLM. You need neurosymbolic architecture - neural networks for the creative generation, symbolic systems for the structural reasoning. Forbes ran a separate analysis of the same revelation. Marcus followed up with a second piece arguing that AlphaFold, AlphaEvolve, AlphaProof, and AlphaGeometry are all neurosymbolic too, and that the field is quietly converging on hybrid approaches even as the public conversation remains fixated on pure LLMs. On Hacker News, a related thread - "You Don't Need Claude Code" - drew debate about whether Claude Code's advantage is the model or the tooling built around it.

Apple is building AI glasses. Mark Gurman's Sunday Power On newsletter reported that Apple is testing at least four different frame styles for smart glasses aimed directly at Meta's Ray-Bans. The designs include both rectangular and oval frames in multiple colours, with a camera system using vertically oriented oval lenses. The glasses will handle photos, video, phone calls, notifications, and music playback - basically an iPhone peripheral you wear on your face. Gurman says Apple could unveil the product as early as late 2026, with sales beginning in 2027. The Verge, TechCrunch, CNET, and Engadget all covered it. This is Apple's next major product category, and the AI angle is central: the glasses are expected to integrate Apple Intelligence for on-device processing. Meta's head start with Ray-Ban Stories is substantial, but Apple's track record of entering categories late and defining them is well-established.

MiniMax open-sourced M2.7, and the headline feature is genuinely novel: the model participated in its own development cycle. MiniMax gave an internal version of M2.7 a programming scaffold and let it run unsupervised on machine learning competitions, then folded the results back into training. It scores 56.22% on SWE-Pro and 57.0% on Terminal Bench 2, and achieves the highest ELO score among open-source models on GDPval-AA. NVIDIA published a technical blog on running it. The "self-evolving agent model" framing is bold, but the concrete results are strong enough that it is more than marketing. For the open-source ecosystem, M2.7 is the most capable openly available agent model right now.
